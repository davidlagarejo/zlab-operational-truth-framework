## 0A.1 Formal definition of operational truth

### Purpose
This section defines the base criterion that will govern what types of assertions can exist within the system. It does not establish an abstract philosophical notion of truth, but rather an operational, usable, and auditable definition for an industrial intelligence system that must produce useful outputs with limited initial data and progressively converge toward higher standards of verification.

### Master definition
A statement is operationally valid within the system when it is supported by traceable evidence, explicit transformation rules, and declared validity conditions, to a degree sufficient to sustain a specific technical use without exceeding the certainty truly justified.

### Governance rule
No claim without traceability, validity conditions, and use-bounded certainty.

### Principle of legitimate exploration
The system must preserve the capacity to generate high-value structural hypotheses under limited evidence, provided that these hypotheses are clearly labeled as exploratory, conditional, and learning-oriented, not confirmation-oriented.

This implies that the system's operational truth is not restricted to already-confirmed knowledge, but does demand explicit separation between:
- sufficiently grounded assertion for a delimited technical use,
- valuable exploratory hypothesis,
- and strong verification.

The framework's function is not to extinguish the capacity to propose plausible mechanisms, structural redesigns, or research lines with limited information. Its function is to prevent those proposals from exploring under the semantics of unearned confirmation.

Consequently, the system can issue exploratory claims when:
- at least one local relevant basis exists,
- the hypothesis is physically or contextually plausible,
- the language is explicitly exploratory,
- and it is declared what additional information would elevate it, degrade it, or discard it.

### Principle of construction-verification duality
Every output constructed by the LLM within the framework must be able to operate in two complementary modes using the same base context:

- **construction mode**: to build an assertion, explanation, hypothesis, table, narrative, diagram, chart, summarized equation, or reporting artifact.
- **verification mode**: to review, stress-test, audit, degrade, correct, or reject that same output using the same context, evidence, constraints, and limits.

The function of this duality is not to duplicate narrative work. Its function is to prevent the LLM from acting only as a persuasive generator without an explicit discipline of contextual self-verification.

Accordingly:
- no serious LLM output should exist only in construction mode,
- every relevant output must be capable of semantic, epistemological, and contextual verification,
- and verification mode must never operate with less context than construction mode.

This applies to:
- claims,
- hypotheses,
- recommendations,
- narrative,
- tables,
- diagrams,
- charts,
- equations,
- and explanatory infographics or artifacts.

### Rule of separation between analysis and formulation
The framework must not treat the LLM as the primary analytical engine for quantitative results, physical inference, predictive modeling, or underlying technical calculation.

The real analysis of the system must rest on:
- machine learning,
- physics,
- simulation,
- explicit rules,
- deterministic calculation,
- statistics,
- and other formally traceable analytical engines.

These engines must not deliver only a textual conclusion. They must first produce a **structured analytical output**, for example:
- tables,
- scores,
- rankings,
- classifications,
- estimates,
- uncertainty bands,
- parameters,
- key variables,
- decision reasons,
- or equivalent structured schemas.

The LLM operates over that structured output in order to:
- structure claims,
- build narrative,
- generate explanation artifacts,
- translate into readable natural language,
- review semantics,
- detect overclaiming,
- and verify fidelity of the output relative to the base context.

Accordingly, the LLM must not present itself as the primary source of analytical truth. Its function is formulation, compression, explanation, and non-destructive contextual verification.

### Thesis of constrained multilayer inference
The framework does not produce useful knowledge by trusting a single family of models, nor does it delegate final interpretation to a single semantic engine. Operational truth emerges when multiple independent layers constrain the space of admissible hypotheses without material contradiction.

These layers do not perform the same function:
- observational design defines what can be observed,
- ML/PIML produces structured inferences,
- physics constrains what is possible,
- statistics quantifies uncertainty,
- the operational model organizes the logic of functioning,
- M&V/IPMVP constrains comparability and defensibility,
- finance constrains viability,
- regulation constrains admissibility,
- the cognitive layer updates beliefs,
- and TAD decides over an already constrained structure.

Within this architecture, the LLM does not establish the system's truth. It only formulates, translates, structures, and semantically verifies outputs already constrained by non-conversational engines and independent restrictions.

### Rule of LLM non-sovereignty
The LLM must not be described or implemented as the primary source of physical, financial, regulatory, or verificatory interpretation.

Its epistemological role is secondary and semantic. The LLM:
- formulates,
- summarizes,
- translates,
- contextualizes,
- drafts,
- constructs explanation artifacts,
- and verifies semantic fidelity of the output.

But it must not:
- determine physical validity by itself,
- determine quantitative uncertainty by itself,
- validate regulatory admissibility by itself,
- nor decide viability or final truth of a claim by itself.

### Rule of structured output precedence
The LLM must not construct material claims, technical recommendations, or explanation artifacts from free intuition when structured output from the analytical engine exists.

When the underlying analysis exists in structured form:
- that output must take precedence over any free reformulation by the LLM,
- the LLM must remain faithful to that structure,
- and all subsequent writing must be traceable back to the original analytical fields.

If sufficient structured output is missing, the system may enter exploratory mode, but it must label that condition explicitly.

### Operative interpretation
This definition implies that the validity of a statement is neither binary nor absolute. It depends on five simultaneous conditions:

1. There must be identifiable and traceable evidence.
2. There must be an explicit rule, method, or transformation between source and statement.
3. Validity conditions under which the statement applies must be declared.
4. There must be a specific technical use for which the statement is sufficient.
5. The strength of language and conclusion cannot exceed the certainty actually supported by available evidence.

Operational truth, therefore, does not equate to total certainty or final verification. It equates to a disciplined way of issuing useful, auditable, and proportionally justified statements.

### Auxiliary definitions

**Operational validity**
Capacity of a statement to be used legitimately within a defined context, given its level of evidence, transformation rules, and stated limits.

**Decisional utility**
Capacity of a statement to materially reduce uncertainty and allow prioritization, dismissal, sequencing, or activation of additional investigation, even if verification has not yet been achieved.

**Verifiability**
Property of a statement that has an explicit path to be confirmed or refuted through additional evidence or reproducible contrast.

**Auditability**
Capacity of a competent third party to reconstruct the source, transformation, assumptions, limits, and issuance criterion of the claim.

**Epistemological degradation**
Loss of category or strength of a statement when higher-order conflicting evidence appears, a validity condition fails, the context of use changes, or the evidence no longer sustains its original formulation.

### What this definition allows
Under this definition, the system can issue useful assertions before reaching complete verification, provided that:

- the assertion has an identifiable source,
- the derivation process is explicit,
- the domain of validity is declared,
- the technical use is bounded,
- and the language used is proportional to available evidence.

This allows the system to operate in decision-grade mode without pretending to be verification-grade.

### What this definition does not allow
The following cases cannot be treated as operational truth within the system:

- outputs without reconstructible source,
- statements without explicit transformation rule,
- hypotheses presented as facts,
- benchmarks presented as confirmed local reality,
- simulations not anchored to observable conditions,
- LLM outputs without independent support,
- point figures without context, range, or condition,
- causal conclusions derived only from correlation,
- recommendations that do not explicitly state critical dependencies.

### Use condition
Every operationally valid statement must be tied to a defined technical use. A statement may be sufficient for screening or prioritization and simultaneously insufficient for verification, audit, M&V, or investment support. Therefore, validity depends not only on the content of the claim, but also on the level of exigence of the use for which it is intended.

### Traceability condition
No statement can enter the system as a serious claim if there is no reconstructible path between:

- source,
- transformation,
- assumptions,
- validity conditions,
- and final form of the output.

Without traceability, a statement can exist as an internal exploratory note, but not as a formal system output.

### Semantic honesty condition
The system cannot use stronger language than permitted by the quality, quantity, and consistency of available evidence. This implies that the semantics of the output must be governed by evidence, not by narrative, commercial, or aesthetic convenience of the report.

### Degradation rule
Every statement must be degraded, suspended, or withdrawn when any of the following conditions occur:

1. conflicting evidence of higher hierarchy appears,
2. the original source loses reliability,
3. the operational context changes and the claim falls outside its validity domain,
4. the rule or model used to derive it becomes unacceptable,
5. the claim is intended to be used for a more exigent purpose than that for which it was originally issued.

### Minimum states derived from this definition
Although the complete taxonomy will be defined later, this section establishes four initial minimum states:

**Not admissible**
Statement that does not meet basic requirements of traceability, explicit transformation, or semantic proportionality.

**Operational hypothesis**
Plausible and useful statement for guiding observation, screening, or investigation, but insufficient to support a strong conclusion.

**Decision-grade assertion**
Statement sufficiently supported to prioritize, compare, dismiss options, or define the next technical step.

**Verification-grade assertion**
Statement supported with a level of evidence, consistency, and traceability sufficient to sustain robust validation, technical audit, or verifiable support of higher exigence.

### Normative closure of the section
This definition establishes that the system should not pursue an appearance of certainty, but rather a discipline of proportional assertion. Its function is not to speak with maximum forcefulness, but with maximum epistemological integrity compatible with the actual state of evidence.

The operational truth of the system does not equate to "what seems reasonable," or "what is measured," or "what serves commercially," or "what a model predicted." It equates to a sufficiently grounded, traceable, contextualized, and semantically honest statement for a specific technical use.

## 0A.2 Epistemological units of the system

### Purpose
This section defines the minimal units of knowledge that the system manipulates. Its objective is to prevent observations, data, inferences, hypotheses, constraints, recommendations, and model results from being mixed semantically as if they were equivalent.

Without this separation, traceability becomes false, language becomes contaminated, scores lose meaning, and reports end up mixing evidence with interpretation.

### Central rule
The system must not issue claims, recommendations, or conclusive language based on observations or model outputs without explicit mediation through evidence, rules, and validity conditions.

### Structural principle
Not every piece of information within the system has the same epistemological status. Some units record facts, others structure data, others support assertions, others transform them, others limit them, and still others activate actions. The system must maintain these differences explicitly at all times.

### Claim as the governing unit
The **claim** is the central unit of the system because it constitutes the minimal form of technically governable assertion. It is the only unit that can be classified, traced, degraded, audited, falsified, and directly connected to a recommendation or a verification result.

All evidence, rules, assumptions, constraints, and model outputs exist to support, limit, qualify, degrade, or enable action based on a claim. The system governs claims, not "insights."

The term "insight" should not be used as an epistemological unit of the framework because it does not distinguish whether it is an observation, an inference, a hypothesis, or a recommendation. It can be used as external language in commercial contexts, but not as an internal category of the system.

---

## 1. Observation

### Definition
Primary record of a fact, event, or condition captured without strong analytical inference.

### Example
- Line temperature measured at 14:00.
- Pressure observed in a pipeline during inspection.
- Monthly consumption recorded on a bill.
- Operating schedule declared by the user.

### Do not confuse with
It is not a claim, not an explanation, and not a recommendation.
An observation may contain error, bias, or noise and still remain an observation.

### Role within the system
Observation is the entry point closest to the real world or declared world. It is epistemological raw material, not conclusion.

### Operative rule
An observation should not be used directly as a technical assertion without passing through structuring, contextualization, and evaluation.

---

## 2. Data Point

### Definition
Structured unit of data derived from an observation or documentary input.

### Example
- `steam_pressure_psig = 92`
- `monthly_kWh = 184000`
- `operating_hours_per_week = 112`
- `annual_gas_cost_usd = 48700`

### Do not confuse with
It is not evidence by itself and not a technical conclusion.

### Role within the system
The data point converts a primary record into a structured representation that can enter rules, models, and traceability.

### Operative rule
A data point must retain reference to its origin, unit, date, context, and confidence level if applicable.

---

## 3. Evidence

### Definition
Element used to support, refute, or contextualize a claim.

### Example
- Energy bills used to support an anomalous consumption pattern.
- Historian used to contrast declared operation.
- Sectoral benchmark used as preliminary reference.
- Applicable standard used to refute a disallowed action.

### Do not confuse with
Not every observation or data point is automatically useful evidence.
It only becomes evidence when it enters into relation with an evaluable assertion.

### Role within the system
Evidence is the explicit support of claims. Without evidence, there is no legitimate basis for assertion.

### Operative rule
All evidence must be typified by source, hierarchy, context, and relevance with respect to the claim it supports or limits.

---

## 4. Inference

### Definition
Logical, statistical, physical, or heuristic transformation that connects evidence to a claim.

### Example
- Inference of anomalous base load from billing and operating schedule.
- Inference of probable thermal loss from temperature, pressure, and consumption pattern.
- Inference of prioritization opportunity based on energy intensity and benchmark.

### Do not confuse with
It is not primary evidence and not equivalent to an observed fact.

### Role within the system
Inference translates evidence into technical meaning. It is necessary to construct claims, but must remain clearly separated from the evidence that originates it.

### Operative rule
All inference must declare its rule, method, or transformation logic and must be capable of being questioned or falsified in principle.

---

## 5. Hypothesis

### Definition
Explanatory proposition that is plausible but not yet sufficiently confirmed.

### Example
- Overload could originate from poor purge.
- The observed pattern could be associated with suboptimal control.
- The dominant cause could be out-of-sequence operation.

### Do not confuse with
It is not confirmed diagnosis, not verification, and not established fact.

### Role within the system
Hypothesis guides measurement, diagnosis, and validation design. It serves to focus effort, not to sell certainty.

### Operative rule
All hypothesis must declare:
- what supports it,
- what weakens it,
- what would falsify it,
- and what evidence would be needed to elevate it.

---

## 6. Assumption

### Definition
Condition adopted explicitly due to absence of direct data or modeling necessity.

### Example
- Assumed load factor.
- Assumed operating schedule.
- Assumed equipment efficiency.
- Assumed operational stability between periods.

### Do not confuse with
It is not evidence, not observation, and should not masquerade as known data.

### Role within the system
Assumption allows operation with incomplete information, but introduces dependency and fragility in the analysis.

### Operative rule
All assumption must be labeled, traced, and associated with potential impact on the claim or recommendation.

---

## 7. Constraint

### Definition
Physical, operational, regulatory, economic, or methodological limit that restricts conclusions or actions.

### Example
- Maximum pressure allowed by operation.
- Standard that prevents certain intervention.
- Budget restriction that limits instrumentation.
- Lack of access to historian.
- Impossibility of stopping process to measure.

### Do not confuse with
It is not causal evidence nor explanation of the phenomenon.

### Role within the system
Constraint delimits what can be asserted and what can be done. It can dominate a recommendation even if the technical opportunity exists.

### Operative rule
All relevant constraint must appear explicitly in reasoning and in final recommendation when it affects viability, sequence, or scope.

---

## 8. Rule

### Definition
Explicit transformation, logical criterion, or formal principle that governs a derivation, classification, or internal decision of the system.

### Example
- Energy balance.
- Rule of precedence between sources.
- Screening threshold.
- Epistemological downgrade criterion.
- Rule to pass from decision-grade to verification-grade.

### Do not confuse with
It is not evidence and not a claim.
It is part of the mechanism for production and governance of knowledge.

### Role within the system
Rule makes visible how the system transforms information into assertions, scores, and recommendations.

### Operative rule
All relevant rules must be explicit, versionable, and auditable.

---

## 9. Claim

### Definition
Technically evaluable assertion issued by the system.

### Example
- There is sufficient indication to prioritize review of the steam system on line A.
- The opportunity appears compatible with unverified thermal losses.
- There is insufficient evidence to support verifiable savings in the current state.
- The dominant cause hypothesis remains preliminary.

### Do not confuse with
It is not an observation, not an implicit hypothesis, not a recommendation, and not just technical narrative.

### Role within the system
Claim is the principal unit of the framework. It is the minimal form of assertion that can:
- be evaluated,
- be classified,
- be traced,
- be degraded,
- be audited,
- be falsified,
- and be connected to a recommendation or verification result.

All epistemological architecture of the system must govern claims, not "insights."

### Operative rule
All claim must have at minimum:
- associated evidence,
- explicit rule or transformation,
- validity conditions,
- verbal strength compatible with evidence,
- and possibility of upgrade, downgrade, or falsification.

---

## 10. Model Output

### Definition
Result issued by an analytical, physical, statistical, ML, simulation, or LLM model.

### Example
- Estimated probability of opportunity.
- Prioritization ranking.
- Energy simulation output.
- Risk score.
- Technical text generated by LLM.

### Do not confuse with
It is not primary truth, not verification, and should not be treated as conclusive evidence by itself.

### Role within the system
Model output can support evaluation, ranking, hypothesis, or drafting, but always as a derived element subordinate to rules, evidence, and domain of validity.

### Operative rule
All model output must be labeled as derived, with model version, relevant inputs, limits, and potential conflict with superior evidence.

---

## 11. Recommendation

### Definition
Action proposal issued by the system based on claims, constraints, and operational objective.

### Example
- Prioritize point measurement on line A.
- Do not advance to verification yet.
- Execute purge inspection before advanced modeling.
- Temporarily discard the opportunity due to lack of minimal basis.

### Do not confuse with
It is not proof that the claim is true, nor equivalent to a decision taken.

### Role within the system
Recommendation connects knowledge with action. Its function is not to declare truth, but to indicate the next technically most justified step under the current state of evidence.

### Operative rule
All recommendation must be linked to one or more explicit claims, declare critical dependencies, and make clear what would change it.

---

## 12. Decision

### Definition
Act of selection, commitment, or authorization performed by a human or institutional agent.

### Example
- Approve additional study.
- Invest in instrumentation.
- Execute pilot.
- Discard an opportunity.
- Escalate to technical audit.

### Do not confuse with
It is not a system recommendation.
Decision belongs to the human, team, or organization.

### Role within the system
Decision is the point where system output translates into actual action, budget, sequence, or commitment.

### Operative rule
The system can support decisions, but should not represent itself as the final decision maker.

---

## 13. Verification Result

### Definition
Result of a sufficiently robust contrast process to elevate, close, confirm, or refute a claim under a stronger standard of proof.

### Example
- Savings confirmed within acceptable tolerance.
- Causal hypothesis refuted by measurement.
- Parameter calibrated and accepted.
- Opportunity discarded by field contrast.
- Claim elevated to verification-grade.

### Do not confuse with
It is not preliminary inference nor merely plausible output.

### Role within the system
Verification result represents a higher state of contrast. It is the mechanism that allows closing claims, refuting them, or elevating their technical status.

### Operative rule
All verification result must declare:
- contrast reference,
- method applied,
- consistency level achieved,
- and exact scope of what remains confirmed or refuted.

---

## 14. Exploratory Structural Claim

### Definition
High-value exploratory assertion that proposes a plausible mechanism, a structural opportunity, a deep redesign, or a high-leverage hypothesis under limited evidence, with the aim of opening space for investigation, strategic reconfiguration, or operational redesign.

### Example
- "The company could be organizing its operation around wrong constraints rather than around the dominant physics of the process."
- "There is a plausible structural hypothesis that the problem is not in an isolated piece of equipment but in the complete sequence of operation."
- "It is worth exploring whether the dominant cost does not come from the main asset but from the operational logic surrounding it."

### Do not confuse with
It is not confirmed diagnosis, not verification, not robust quantification, and should not be treated as closed finding.

### Role within the system
The exploratory structural claim preserves the framework's capacity to produce value even under limited information availability. Its function is to open powerful research lines, propose plausible redesigns, and capture technically disciplined structural intuitions without pretending to confirmation.

### Operative rule
Every exploratory structural claim must:
- explicitly declare its exploratory character,
- rest on at least one local relevant signal,
- be compatible with known physical and normative constraints,
- and explicitly state what additional evidence would elevate it, degrade it, or discard it.

---

## 15. Explanation Artifact

### Definition
An explanatory artifact constructed by the system to represent a claim, comparison, mechanism, structure, projection, conflict, or verification result through a table, chart, diagram, annotated image, summarized formula, or equivalent visual composition.

### Example
- table of signals, impact, and confidence,
- baseline vs current vs projected chart,
- operational sequence flowchart,
- exploratory causal map,
- system boundary diagram,
- summarized equation with relevant parameters,
- prioritization matrix.

### Do not confuse with
It is not primary evidence. It is not a claim by itself. It is not automatic verification. It is a derived and governed representation.

### Role within the system
The explanation artifact exists to increase understanding, clarity, and communication capacity without artificially increasing the epistemological strength of the claim it represents.

When structured analytical output exists, the explanation artifact must preferably be constructed from that structured output rather than from free LLM generation.

### Operative rule
Every explanation artifact must:
- be linked to one or more explicit claims,
- inherit the epistemological level of the claims it represents,
- declare its purpose,
- be capable of being constructed and verified using the same base context,
- and not introduce relations, entities, or closures not present in the claim pack.

## 16. Structured Analytical Output

### Definition
Structured output produced by the system's formal analytical engines, such as machine learning, physics, simulation, statistics, or explicit calculation, containing results, signals, scores, classifications, parameters, uncertainties, or technical reasons in a governable and traceable format.

### Example
- opportunity score table,
- risk ranking,
- anomaly classification,
- estimate with uncertainty range,
- dominant-variable matrix,
- JSON with results and key reasons,
- parameters calculated by a physical model.

### Do not confuse with
It is not final narrative, not an explanation artifact, not primary evidence by itself, and not an already-written claim. It is the intermediate structured form between formal analysis and communicable formulation.

### Role within the system
Structured analytical output acts as a disciplined interface between:
- the underlying analytical engines,
- and the LLM as a formulation and verification layer.

### Operative rule
Every material output written by the LLM should, whenever possible, be reconstructible from an explicit structured analytical output.

## 17. Constrained Interpretation

### Definition
Interpretation resulting from convergence between multiple independent layers of restriction over the same problem, where no individual model imposes the final meaning of the output by itself.

### Example
- a hypothesis that survives physical consistency, statistical support, operational coherence, regulatory admissibility, and economic viability simultaneously;
- a recommendation that not only sounds narratively reasonable, but is also compatible with heterogeneous restrictions.

### Do not confuse with
It is not LLM narrative, not a single ML inference, not a free explanation, and not a final decision.

### Role within the system
Constrained Interpretation represents the epistemologically most important point of the framework: useful output does not come from a single layer, but from the intersection between independent restrictions.

### Operative rule
A material claim must not be presented as well interpreted if it has only been processed by a semantic layer or by a single family of models. It must be traceable to a structure of convergent restrictions.

### Design note on artifacts
The framework must not treat tables, charts, diagrams, or summarized equations as report decoration. It must treat them as governed explanatory artifacts, subject to the same traceability rules, semantic limits, and contextual verification as textual claims.

## Minimal hierarchical flow
Observation → Data Point → Evidence → Inference / Assumption / Constraint / Rule → Structured Analytical Output → Constrained Interpretation → Claim / Exploratory Structural Claim → Explanation Artifact / Recommendation → Decision / Verification Result

---

## Rule of semantic non-mixing
The system must prevent the following confusions:

- calling an inference "data,"
- calling a hypothesis a "finding,"
- calling LLM output "evidence" without external support,
- calling screening a "diagnosis,"
- calling a recommendation a "decision,"
- calling an unvalidated simulation "verification."

These confusions are not merely language errors; they are epistemological architecture errors.

---

## Normative closure of the section
The system must operate on explicit epistemological units and not on vague narrative categories. The word "insight" can be used in external communication if convenient, but internally has no governance value.

The central unit of the framework is the **claim**. Everything else exists to originate it, support it, limit it, transform it, degrade it, or enable action based on it.

## 0A.3 Operative taxonomy of knowledge

### Purpose
This section defines the formal taxonomy of epistemological status used to classify claims within the system. It does not classify types of epistemological unit, but rather the knowledge state of each claim according to dominant origin, mode of production, and degree of contrast achieved.

Its function is to prevent claims of different robustness from appearing equivalent, and to govern in a disciplined manner:
- the permitted language,
- the permitted technical use,
- traceability,
- upgrade and downgrade,
- and the eligibility of each claim to enter a serious report.

### Structural rule
The taxonomy should not operate as a flat list of mixed labels. The system must classify each claim in three separate dimensions:

1. **Source Status**: where the dominant support originates.
2. **Knowledge Mode**: how the claim was produced.
3. **Validation Status**: what degree of contrast was achieved.

### Operative rule
No serious claim should remain classified with a single simple label. Every claim must record, at minimum:
- `source_status`
- `knowledge_mode`
- `validation_status`

When applicable, the same claim may have more than one label within the same dimension, provided that it honestly reflects its real support structure.

---

## A. Source Status

### 1. Measured

**Definition**
Primary support based on direct measurement or instrumental or primary documentary observation.

**Example**
- pressure sensor reading,
- operational historian,
- energy bill,
- point measurement in field,
- primary documentary record issued by the facility.

**Entry criterion**
There exists an identifiable, locatable, and traceable primary source that records a condition or variable of the real system.

**Exit criterion**
It ceases to be the dominant source when the claim becomes materially dependent on analytical transformation, modeling, or assumptions rather than the primary evidence itself.

**Expected reliability**
Medium to high, depending on instrument quality, capture context, data integrity, and possible operational bias.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Instrumental error, poor calibration, drift, capture out of context, unrepresentative period, or correct reading of an irrelevant variable for the claim.

**Permitted use**
Strong foundation for descriptive claims, empirical contrast, falsification, and verification.

---

### 2. User-Declared

**Definition**
Support based on explicit input from the user, operator, or human actor reporting conditions, constraints, schedules, sequences, or configurations.

**Example**
- declared operating hours,
- operational sequence reported by operator,
- access restriction declared by plant,
- shift schedule reported by client.

**Entry criterion**
The information comes from an identifiable human source and is recorded as an explicit declaration.

**Exit criterion**
It is degraded as dominant source if superior conflicting evidence appears, or if the declaration is inconsistent with other internal sources.

**Expected reliability**
Low to medium.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Memory bias, over-simplification, human error, incomplete perception, incentive to protect prior decisions, or partial ignorance of real system.

**Permitted use**
Initial context, screening, assumptions, preliminary operational constraints, early prioritization.

---

### 3. Expert Prior

**Definition**
Support derived from previously formalized expert knowledge applicable to a delimited technical domain.

**Example**
- engineering rule based on documented experience,
- reasonable physical prior,
- formalized expert criterion for pattern classification,
- structured expert judgment about typical process behavior.

**Entry criterion**
There exists an explicit expert source, a defined domain of use, and justification of applicability.

**Exit criterion**
It ceases to dominate when stronger local evidence enters or when it is demonstrated that the context is not sufficiently comparable.

**Expected reliability**
Medium.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Over-generalization, experience bias, transferring patterns valid in one context to another where they no longer apply.

**Permitted use**
Hypothesis initialization, technical priors, screening, test design, early interpretation.

---

### 4. Benchmark-Based

**Definition**
Support based on comparative external reference with respect to peers, sectors, typologies, intensities, or typical behaviors.

**Example**
- sectoral energy intensity benchmark,
- typical consumption curve per process,
- comparative performance ratio,
- performance reference of similar assets.

**Entry criterion**
There exists an identifiable benchmark and minimal justification of comparability with the evaluated installation or system.

**Exit criterion**
It is degraded when comparability is weak, the benchmark is too generic, or local measurement contradicts it.

**Expected reliability**
Low to medium for local claims; medium for comparative screening.

**Primary epistemological risk**
False equivalence between installations, processes, or non-comparable operational conditions.

**Permitted use**
Screening, prioritization, preliminary contextualization, comparative deviation estimation.

---

### 5. Literature-Supported

**Definition**
Support derived from published technical or scientific literature.

**Example**
- peer-reviewed paper,
- technical guideline,
- reference manual,
- methodological standard,
- published technical document on causal behavior or operational limits.

**Entry criterion**
There exists an explicit, traceable, and relevant reference for the type of claim or mechanism being evaluated.

**Exit criterion**
It is degraded as local support if it contradicts strong empirical evidence in the specific case or if the context of the literature is not sufficiently comparable.

**Expected reliability**
Medium.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Undue extrapolation, limited applicability, results obtained in conditions that do not represent the real facility.

**Permitted use**
Technical contextualization, plausible causality, test design, hypothesis support, methodological delimitation.

---

## B. Knowledge Mode

### 6. Direct

**Definition**
The claim is almost descriptive and depends minimally on analytical transformation.

**Example**
- "Annual consumed electricity was 184,000 kWh."
- "Recorded line pressure was 92 psig."
- "Declared operation corresponds to 112 hours per week."

**Entry criterion**
The claim reproduces or synthesizes almost immediately from primary evidence, without complex interpretation.

**Exit criterion**
It ceases to be direct when it incorporates thresholds, causal relationships, complex comparisons, or relevant technical interpretation.

**Expected reliability**
High if primary source is good.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Believing that a direct claim is context-free simply because it lacks complex transformation.

**Permitted use**
Factual description, documentary foundation, descriptive reports, inputs for subsequent reasoning.

---

### 7. Derived

**Definition**
The claim results from an explicit, reproducible, and traceable transformation over available evidence.

**Example**
- calculated energy intensity,
- derived unit cost,
- preliminary energy balance,
- consumption ratio per unit produced,
- calculated thermal delta.

**Entry criterion**
There exists an explicit rule or calculation applied over traceable data.

**Exit criterion**
It is degraded if inputs are too weak, if hidden assumptions appear, or if the rule ceases to apply to the context.

**Expected reliability**
Medium to high, depending on input quality and transformation validity.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Calculation error, undeclared implicit assumption, false precision, or correct transformation over incorrect foundation.

**Permitted use**
Comparative analysis, preliminary quantification, robust screening, preparation for validation.

---

### 8. Inferred

**Definition**
The claim results from physical, statistical, logical, or heuristic inference that interprets evidence to produce a technically evaluable assertion.

**Example**
- "There is sufficient indication of probable thermal loss."
- "The observed pattern is compatible with suboptimal control."
- "The operational sequence suggests a prioritization opportunity."

**Entry criterion**
There exists explicit evidence, an identifiable inference rule or logic, and a technically formulated claim.

**Exit criterion**
It is elevated with additional contrast or degraded if superior conflict appears, lack of domain, or more consistent alternative inference.

**Expected reliability**
Medium.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Confusing plausibility with fact, or converting strong inference into confirmed diagnosis without sufficient contrast.

**Permitted use**
Prioritization, advanced screening, causal hypothesis, preliminary diagnosis, measurement design.

---

### 9. Simulated

**Definition**
The claim is substantially dependent on simulation, mechanistic modeling, or projected behavior of a structural or computational model.

**Example**
- estimated savings under an operational scenario,
- expected system response under new sequence,
- projected consumption with setpoint change,
- simulated thermal behavior.

**Entry criterion**
There exists explicit model, documented inputs, visible assumptions, and material relationship between model output and issued claim.

**Exit criterion**
It is degraded if there is insufficient calibration, if inputs fall outside the valid domain, or if real behavior systematically contradicts the simulation.

**Expected reliability**
Low to medium without calibration; medium to high if well calibrated and within domain.

**Primary epistemological risk**
False precision, excessive assumption dependency, illusion of robustness from mathematical sophistication.

**Permitted use**
Scenarios, sensitivity, test design, conditional quantification, exploration of behavior not directly observed.

---

### 10. Model-Assisted

**Definition**
The claim is produced or significantly supported by an analytical, statistical, ML, or LLM model, but does not depend exclusively on it.

**Example**
- opportunity ranking,
- prioritization score,
- model-assisted classification,
- technical draft generated from structured evidence,
- pattern suggested by predictive analytics.

**Entry criterion**
The model materially influences the claim or its formulation, but there also exists non-model-based support or additional rules accompanying it.

**Exit criterion**
It is degraded if the model inappropriately dominates against superior evidence, if its behavior is opaque, or if it acts outside domain.

**Expected reliability**
Variable.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Opacity, bias, overconfidence, undue authority of outputs that appear coherent but are not sufficiently grounded.

**Permitted use**
Assisted prioritization, preliminary classification, comparative support, controlled technical drafting, support for human or hybrid inferences.

---

### 11. Assumption-Dependent

**Definition**
The claim is materially dependent on one or more assumptions not directly observed or verified.

**Example**
- "Preliminary annual savings depends on assuming 6,000 hours/year."
- "Estimated opportunity depends on assuming stable thermal load."
- "Prioritization score depends on assuming continuous operation."

**Entry criterion**
Reasonable changes in one or more assumptions materially alter the value, status, or recommendation derived from the claim.

**Exit criterion**
It loses this label when critical assumptions are replaced by observed data or bounded with sufficient evidence.

**Expected reliability**
Low to medium.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Structural fragility of the claim, hidden dependency, and false appearance of robustness.

**Permitted use**
Screening, scenarios, conditional prioritization, exploratory analysis, explicitly bounded claims by assumptions.

**Special rule**
`Assumption-Dependent` is a mandatory label when applicable. It cannot be omitted for narrative convenience.

---

### 12. Exploratory

**Definition**
The claim is issued in exploratory mode to propose a structural hypothesis, a high-leverage opportunity, or a plausible redesign under limited evidence, without pretending confirmed diagnosis or verification.

**Example**
- proposal for deep operational redesign based on initial signals,
- hypothesis that dominant inefficiency resides in operation architecture rather than in a single piece of equipment,
- positing of a structural opportunity not yet obvious or confirmed.

**Entry criterion**
There is at least one local relevant signal, a basis of physical, contextual, or expert plausibility, and explicit justification for why the hypothesis merits exploration.

**Exit criterion**
It degrades if no explicit learning route exists or if it contradicts physical or normative constraints.

**Expected reliability**
Low to medium, depending on local signaling and theoretical plausibility.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Allowing exploration to masquerade as confirmed diagnosis.

**Permitted use**
Strategic redesign, high-leverage hypothesis, research lines, audacious structural propositions under low information, but always labeled as exploratory.

**Special rule**
`Exploratory` is a label that can only be used if explicitly declared as such in the claim. It cannot be hidden under stronger language. Legitimate exploration does not eliminate rigor; it displaces the claim to a semantically honest category.

---

## C. Validation Status

### 13. Unvalidated

**Definition**
The claim has not been contrasted beyond internal coherence, basic plausibility, or preliminary support.

**Example**
- claim issued with benchmark and declared schedules, but without sufficient local empirical contrast,
- preliminary inference sustained by initial evidence, still without cross-validation,
- estimation based on uncalibrated simulation.

**Entry criterion**
The claim exists and is traced, but has not received sufficient material contrast against additional evidence, strong reference, or specific test.

**Exit criterion**
It passes to validated when it receives partial contrast, relevant convergent evidence, or material validation of method and context.

**Expected reliability**
Variable, normally low to medium.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Premature language inflation, misuse in contexts requiring higher standard of proof.

**Permitted use**
Internal, screening, limited prioritization, hypothesis, design of next technical step.

---

### 14. Validated

**Definition**
The claim has received partial or sufficient contrast to support more robust technical utility, but still does not reach complete verification closure.

**Example**
- consistency against multiple independent sources,
- partial field contrast,
- reasonable validation of a transformation or method,
- material uncertainty reduction without conclusive final proof.

**Entry criterion**
There exists additional evidence or sufficient contrast to materially reduce uncertainty and support a more robust technical action or judgment.

**Exit criterion**
It rises to verified with harder, reproducible, and auditable proof; it falls if superior conflict appears or if validation proved too limited for intended use.

**Expected reliability**
Medium to high.

**Primary epistemological risk**
Over-extending partial validation as if it were already closed verification.

**Permitted use**
Strong preliminary diagnosis, robust prioritization, conditional decision to proceed, intermediate technical support.

---

### 15. Verified

**Definition**
The claim has been contrasted under a strong, reproducible, and auditable standard, sufficient to support a high-weight assertion within its domain of validity.

**Example**
- measurement and contrast within explicit tolerance,
- reproducible confirmation of a technical assertion,
- dominant alternative hypothesis refuted,
- robust field verification result,
- claim elevated with strong evidence and complete traceability.

**Entry criterion**
There exists explicit verification method, strong evidence, delimited validity domain, and auditable reconstruction of the contrast performed.

**Exit criterion**
Only falls if methodological error is discovered, superior conflict appears, material context change occurs, or use falls outside domain.

**Expected reliability**
High.

**Primary epistemological risk**
False sense of universality: believing verified means general truth rather than strong truth within a delimited domain.

**Permitted use**
Strong report, audit, serious investment decision support, high-weight technical validation, M&V or other demanding uses according to scope.

---

## Critical note on Validated and Verified
`Validated` and `Verified` are not equivalent.

- **Validated** implies material contrast, still partial, limited, or insufficient for complete verification closure.
- **Verified** implies a higher standard of proof, reproducible and auditable.

Collapsing both categories would destroy the distinction between robust technical utility and strong confirmation. That distinction is mandatory within the framework.

---

## Multiple labeling rule
Each claim must record its complete taxonomic combination.

### Example 1
**Claim:** "There is sufficient indication to prioritize review of the steam system."

- `source_status = Measured + Benchmark-Based`
- `knowledge_mode = Inferred`
- `validation_status = Unvalidated`

**Reading**
It is useful for prioritization, but not for asserting confirmed cause or verified savings.

### Example 2
**Claim:** "Estimated specific consumption is 18% higher than comparable benchmark."

- `source_status = Measured + Benchmark-Based`
- `knowledge_mode = Derived`
- `validation_status = Validated`

**Reading**
There exists preliminary consistent quantification, but its strength still depends on comparability and context.

### Example 3
**Claim:** "Projected annual savings critically depends on assuming 6,000 hours/year."

- `source_status = User-Declared`
- `knowledge_mode = Derived + Assumption-Dependent`
- `validation_status = Unvalidated`

**Reading**
The figure can guide screening, but is fragile and should not be sold as robust estimation.

### Example 4
**Claim:** "The hypothesis of dominant purge loss was refuted by field measurement."

- `source_status = Measured`
- `knowledge_mode = Direct + Inferred`
- `validation_status = Verified`

**Reading**
The assertion has already passed sufficient strong contrast to sustain refutation within its domain.

## Additional operative dimension: Output Mode

In addition to:
- `source_status`
- `knowledge_mode`
- `validation_status`

every claim or explanation artifact constructed by the LLM must also register:

- `output_mode = construction`
- `output_mode = verification`

### Definition
`construction` indicates that the system is building a formulation, representation, or artifact from the available context.

`verification` indicates that the system is auditing, stress-testing, or reviewing a previously constructed output using the same base context, with the objective of detecting:
- overclaiming,
- context errors,
- semantic distortion,
- contradiction with constraints,
- representation errors,
- or epistemological inflation.

### Operative rule
No serious LLM output should remain only in `construction` if it is going to enter the system as a relevant claim, recommendation, or reporting explanation artifact.

### Critical note
`verification` does not automatically equal `Verified`. The output mode describes the function of the LLM in that step, not the final epistemological status of the claim.

## Analytical interface rule
Before the LLM formulates a serious claim, recommendation, or material explanation artifact, the system must prefer a structured analytical output coming from the corresponding analytical engine.

The correct sequence is:
1. analytical engine produces structured analytical output,
2. LLM formulates or explains,
3. verification mode checks semantic fidelity and contextual integrity.

This sequence must not be inverted for material claims except in explicit exploratory mode.

## Rule of epistemological convergence
The robustness of a claim must not be inferred only from:
- narrative elegance,
- visual intensity,
- apparent model sophistication,
- or the presence of isolated structured analytical output.

It must be inferred from the degree of convergence between independent restrictions.

This implies that a claim rises in solidity when:
- structured analysis supports it,
- physics does not contradict it,
- statistics does not render it fragile,
- operation does not render it absurd,
- regulation does not invalidate it,
- finance does not render it unviable,
- and new evidence does not force belief revision.

## Closed design decisions

The following decisions are adopted for the framework:

1. Operative taxonomy of knowledge is implemented in three separate dimensions:
   - `source_status`
   - `knowledge_mode`
   - `validation_status`

2. `Model-Assisted` and `Simulated` remain as distinct categories.
   - `Simulated` applies when the claim is substantially dependent on simulation or model behavior.
   - `Model-Assisted` applies when the claim is significantly supported by an analytical, ML, or LLM model, but does not depend exclusively on it.

3. `Assumption-Dependent` is a mandatory label when one or more unobserved assumptions materially affect the claim.

4. `Validated` and `Verified` remain as formally distinct states.
   - `Validated` implies partial or sufficient contrast for more robust technical utility, but not complete verification closure.
   - `Verified` implies contrast under a strong, reproducible, and auditable standard.

## Design note on exploration

The addition of `Exploratory` as a knowledge mode (item 12) formalizes a capacity the framework must preserve: the generation of valuable structural hypotheses, deep redesigns, and high-leverage claims even under limited information.

This category does not authorize rhetorical speculation nor does it collapse rigor. Its function is to:
- Recognize that disciplined exploration is a system value.
- Prevent valuable ideas from dying from excessive notarial rigor.
- Demand clear separation: exploratory ≠ confirmed.
- Maintain semantic honesty in every exploratory claim.

An exploratory claim must declare:
- Its explicitly exploratory character.
- At least one local signal or basis of plausibility.
- What information would elevate it, degrade it, or discard it.
- Physical and normative constraints it respects.

Without these declarations, the claim is not exploration; it is uncontrolled speculation.

## Anti-asphyxia clause
The framework is designed to prevent overclaiming, not to suppress disciplined exploration.

Exploratory claims, decision-grade outputs, structural hypotheses, and early recommendations are admissible when they are:
- properly tagged,
- bounded,
- traced,
- and governed by proportional grammar,

even if they do not yet satisfy stronger Verification-grade requirements or the heavier controls reserved for high-impact material outputs.

Governance burden must scale with:
- epistemic ambition,
- output visibility,
- overread risk,
- and cost of error.

Not every useful early output should carry the heaviest machinery of the framework.

---

## Normative closure
The taxonomy exists to prevent claims of different robustness from appearing equivalent. Its function is to govern language, permitted use, upgrade, downgrade, traceability, and reportability eligibility.

The system must not tolerate flat labels that hide the real structure of support for a claim. Every serious assertion must be characterized by its dominant source, its mode of production, and its achieved degree of contrast.

---

### Cross-cutting note on LLM duality
The framework does not assume an LLM only as a generator. It assumes an LLM capable of operating in two disciplined functions: construction and verification.

### Cross-cutting note on analytical separation
The framework does not assume the LLM as the primary engine of quantitative analysis, predictive modeling, or physical inference. That function belongs to machine learning, physics, simulation, statistics, explicit calculation, and other formal analytical engines.

### Cross-cutting note on structured output
When formal analysis exists, the system must prefer structured analytical outputs as the interface between the analytical engine and LLM formulation.

### Cross-cutting note on non-destruction
No relevant system output should depend exclusively on persuasive generation that has not been audited against the same base context that originated it, nor on destructive verification with the power to delete, replace, or publish without external control.

### Cross-cutting note on constrained inference
The framework does not entrust operational truth to a single model. Claim validity emerges when multiple independent layers constrain the space of hypotheses without material contradiction.

### Cross-cutting note on the role of the LLM
The LLM is not the epistemological center of the system. Its function is semantic: to formulate, translate, structure, explain, and verify the narrative fidelity of outputs already constrained by non-conversational engines.

### Cross-cutting note on convergence
Hallucination reduction is not an isolated property of the LLM. It is a property of architecture, produced by the intersection of physics, statistics, operation, M&V, regulation, finance, belief updating, and semantic control.
