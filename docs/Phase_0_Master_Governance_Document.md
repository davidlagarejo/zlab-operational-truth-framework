# Phase 0 — Master Document of Operational Truth and Epistemic Governance

## 1. Purpose and role of Phase 0
Phase 0 defines the system's operative constitution. Its function is to govern how claims, outputs, recommendations, and artifacts are born, limited, formulated, reviewed, traced, degraded, and admitted before any later phase of deployment, automation, or scaling.

Phase 0 does not exist to produce artificial closure. It exists to:
- turn heterogeneous support into technically useful intelligence,
- allow disciplined exploration under incomplete information,
- prevent hallucination, false precision, and invented causality,
- and establish when an output may be used, shown, limited, rebuilt, blocked, or rejected.

It does not replace physics, statistics, simulation, measurement, regulation, or technical judgment. It governs the relation between those layers and the system's visible output.

### Canonical normative hierarchy
The framework's control hierarchy must be read in this order:
1. non-negotiable laws,
2. critical distinctions,
3. operational rules,
4. binary admissibility gates,
5. auxiliary scores,
6. possible outcomes,
7. traced human override.

Not every statement carries the same normative weight.

### Non-negotiable rule
Phase 0 governs epistemic admissibility, not documentary ornament.

### Failure mode to prevent
That the system produce useful language but epistemologically opaque outputs, or governance so heavy that it destroys Decision-grade and legitimate exploration.

## 1.1 Authority of the Master Document
This Master Document is the canonical source of Phase 0 governance.

It performs three formal roles:
1. superior interpretive authority for the full Phase 0 corpus;

## 2. Foundational epistemic thesis
The entire Phase 0 architecture is governed by these laws:

1. **No single model is trusted with truth.**
2. **Truth emerges from constrained agreement across independent layers.**
3. Operational truth emerges from constrained multilayer inference, not from a single source, a single score, or a single narrative.
4. The system restricts hypotheses more than it "knows."
5. The LLM is not sovereign.

Real analysis belongs to:
- machine learning,
- physics,
- simulation,
- statistics,
- explicit rules,
- deterministic calculation,
- and other formal analytical engines.

These engines first produce **structured analytical output**. The LLM:
- formulates,
- summarizes,
- explains,
- structures,
- translates,
- and semantically verifies,
on top of that base.

### Critical distinction
Structured analytical output is structured analytical support. LLM output is governed formulation over that support. They must not collapse into each other.

### Non-negotiable rule
Hallucination reduction is an architectural property, not a property of the isolated LLM.

## 3. Core epistemic units
The framework's core units are:
- observation,
- data point,
- evidence,
- inference,
- hypothesis,
- assumption,
- constraint,
- rule,
- claim,
- model output,
- recommendation,
- decision,
- verification result,
- exploratory structural claim,
- explanation artifact,
- structured analytical output,
- and constrained interpretation.

### Compact operative definitions
- `observation`: situated record of a phenomenon, condition, or measurement.
- `data point`: punctual unit of data without strong interpretation by itself.
- `evidence`: data or observation already situated within a support relation.
- `inference`: traceable interpretive step between base and conclusion.
- `hypothesis`: explanatory or structural assertion not yet closed.
- `assumption`: condition provisionally accepted to operate.
- `constraint`: physical, statistical, normative, operative, or methodological limit restricting interpretation.
- `rule`: explicit system transformation or criterion.
- `claim`: governing unit of technically traceable assertion.
- `model output`: output of a formal engine or model, not equivalent to final claim.
- `recommendation`: conduct guidance dependent on claim base and change condition.
- `decision`: act external to the framework; the system may support it, not replace it.
- `verification result`: outcome of delimited contrast, not synonym of universal truth.
- `exploratory structural claim`: useful structural hypothesis under partial restriction.
- `explanation artifact`: visual, tabular, or multimodal interpretation surface.
- `structured analytical output`: structured output produced by formal analytical engines.
- `constrained interpretation`: formulation subjected to explicit restrictions, not free reading.

### Critical distinction
Not every output is a claim. Not every artifact is evidence. Not every recommendation is a decision.

## 4. Claim-centered governance architecture
The claim is the central governing unit because it concentrates:
- asserted content,
- intended use,
- available support,
- domain of validity,
- epistemic taxonomy,
- traceability,
- and downstream effects on outputs and artifacts.

### Mandatory taxonomic dimensions
Every serious claim must declare at minimum:
- `source_status`
- `knowledge_mode`
- `validation_status`
- `output_mode`

These dimensions are not interchangeable.

### Critical distinctions
- `source_status` describes origin of support.
- `knowledge_mode` describes how it is known.
- `validation_status` describes epistemic state of the claim.
- `output_mode` describes how the LLM participates in formulation or review.

`construction mode` and `verification mode` are LLM operative modes.  
`Validated` and `Verified` are epistemic states of the claim.  
They must not collapse.

### Non-negotiable rule
A claim does not exist because it was formulated. It exists while it preserves sufficient support, traceability, coherence, domain, and permitted use.

## 5. Evidence regime
Evidence strength is not an isolated property of data. It is a property of its position within a structure of restriction, contrast, traceability, and use.

### Operative hierarchy
- Strong local evidence prevails over benchmark, literature, analogy, or expert judgment, unless demonstrably compromised.
- Physical and regulatory restrictions operate as limit-evidence.
- Explanation artifacts do not increase epistemic strength of the claim.
- Structured analytical output does not replace multilayer convergence by itself.

### Real modifiers of strength
Evidence strength is modified by:
- contextual quality and relevance,
- traceability,
- comparability,
- open conflict,
- dependence on assumptions,
- physical consistency,
- operational consistency,
- and sufficiency for intended use.

### Conflict policy
The system does not artificially reconcile conflicts. In the face of conflict it may:
- resolve by explicit precedence,
- degrade,
- suspend,
- block dependent output,
- or escalate.

### Sufficiency rule
Sufficiency depends on intended use and claim type. Decision-grade requires sufficiency to orient serious action. Verification-grade requires stronger multilayer convergence to support high-weight technical assertion within a delimited scope.

### Clause of legitimate exploration
Low evidence does not prohibit exploration. It obliges labeling, delimitation, and proportional formulation.

### Failure mode to prevent
Requiring strong verification standard for every useful early claim.

## 6. Semantic regime
Every formulation must obey strict semantic proportionality: verbal strength may not exceed epistemic strength of the support.

### Core rules
- unearned absolutism prohibited,
- closed causality without sufficient contrast prohibited,
- universality outside domain prohibited,
- unearned numeric precision prohibited,
- empty authority prohibited,
- ornamental depth prohibited,
- narrative that hides conflict or dominant assumptions prohibited.

### Legitimate levels of verbal strength
The system admits, as general families, formulations that are:
- descriptive,
- indicative,
- hypothetical-causal,
- conditional-quantitative,
- exploratory-structural,
- and delimited-confirmatory.

### Non-negotiable rule
Even a strong claim must sound delimited, not absolute.

### Critical distinction
Good writing does not promote a claim. It only prevents misrepresentation of its real level.

## 7. Operational regimes

### 7.1 Decision-grade
Decision-grade is the regime in which multilayer restriction is already sufficient to orient serious action, prioritization, measurement, discarding, or useful next step, even though the strong convergence required by Verification-grade does not yet exist.

It authorizes:
- serious screening,
- prioritization,
- preliminary recommendations,
- valuable structural hypotheses,
- conditional projections,
- and disciplined technical guidance.

It does not authorize:
- strong technical closure,
- universality,
- unearned closed causality,
- nor high-weight recommendation without mediation.

### 7.2 Verification-grade
Verification-grade is the regime of high restricted convergence across independent layers. It exists when strong evidence, sufficient structured analytical output, explicit contrast method, delimited domain, high traceability, and absence of unresolved material conflict allow support of high-weight technical assertion within a defined scope.

It is not equivalent to universal truth.

### 7.3 Transition rule
The transition from Decision-grade to Verification-grade occurs only when the claim's epistemic support changes materially:
- new or stronger evidence,
- better structured analytical output,
- explicit contrast method,
- greater multilayer convergence,
- lower dependence on critical assumptions,
- lower conflict,
- and higher traceability.

More presentation is not more verification.

Promotion may be:
- partial,
- localized,
- reversible.

### 7.4 LLM operational duality
Every material LLM output must be able to exist as:
- `construction mode`
- `verification mode`

`construction mode` formulates.  
`verification mode` judges semantic fidelity, proportionality, limits, clarity, and risk of overassertion.  
Neither one by itself replaces epistemic validation of the underlying claim.

`verification mode`:
- uses the same base context,
- does not directly edit the original,
- and may only approve, approve with warning, degrade, reject, or request explicit correction.

### Non-negotiable rule
LLM verification mode does not automatically equal `Verified`.

## 8. Traceability and object governance
Every serious material output must exist as a traceable object. Traceability is not only a log. It is log + technical sheet + state history + dependency tree + audit record.

### Minimum object families
- `Claim Object`
- `Output Object`
- `Artifact Object`

### Structural rules
- No material output enters as free final text.
- No material artifact enters as visual without a record.
- Every object must allow reconstruction of what it said or showed, where it came from, what limits it, what review it received, and what would degrade or invalidate it.

### Inheritance between objects
Outputs and artifacts inherit from the base claim:
- domain,
- limits,
- conflict,
- restrictions,
- critical assumptions,
- and degradation possibility.

No output or artifact may have greater epistemic strength than the claim on which it depends.

### Claim lifecycle
Every serious claim must be governed through `lifecycle_status`:
- `Drafted`
- `Admitted`
- `Active`
- `Promoted`
- `Degraded`
- `Suspended`
- `Rejected`
- `Retired`
- `Reactivated`

This does not replace `validation_status`.

### Separate numeric dimensions
The framework may use:
- `lifecycle_state_code` for system logic and transitions,
- `epistemic_strength_score` for epistemic strength,
- `actionability_score` for decision utility,
- and auxiliary scores such as `conflict_penalty` or `convergence_score`.

These dimensions must not collapse into a single number.

### Governance of outputs and artifacts
Outputs and artifacts have their own governance because the reader does not see abstract claims: the reader sees tables, charts, captions, executive paragraphs, diagrams, and concrete recommendations.

A claim may remain alive and yet:
- its output may be blocked,
- its artifact may be retired,
- its caption may require rebuild,
- or its representation may become inadmissible.

### Minimum derived states
Outputs:
- `Drafted`
- `Reviewed`
- `Approved`
- `Approved_with_Limits`
- `Blocked`
- `Deprecated`
- `Retired`
- `Historical`
- `Rebuilt`

Artifacts:
- `Drafted`
- `Reviewed`
- `Approved`
- `Approved_with_Limits`
- `Blocked`
- `Retired`
- `Historical`
- `Rebuilt`

### Rule of automatic reevaluation
If the base claim changes state, the system must automatically reevaluate dependent outputs and artifacts.

### Rule of non-zombification
No non-active claim may continue circulating materially through outputs or artifacts as if it preserved full admissibility.

### Rules of state change
The system admits:
- upgrade,
- downgrade,
- suspend,
- reject,
- rebuild,
- retire.

It must apply minimum epistemically honest intervention:
1. limit / warning
2. downgrade
3. suspend
4. rebuild
5. reject
6. retire

Do not reject if downgrade is enough.  
Do not downgrade if limiting is enough.  
Do not keep active if it already should be suspended.

### Canonical model of visible states and outcomes
To avoid nominal collapse across layers, the framework separates:
- `validation_status`: `Unvalidated`, `Validated`, `Verified`;
- `lifecycle_status`: `Drafted`, `Admitted`, `Active`, `Promoted`, `Degraded`, `Suspended`, `Rejected`, `Retired`, `Reactivated`;
- `publication_status`: `Drafted`, `Reviewed`, `Approved`, `Approved_with_Limits`, `Blocked`, `Deprecated`, `Retired`, `Historical`, `Rebuilt`;
- `review_outcome`: `Pass`, `Pass_with_Warning`, `Limit_Required`, `Rebuild_Required`, `Reject`, `Human_Review_Required`;
- `admissibility_decision`: `Approved`, `Approved_with_Warning`, `Limit_Scope`, `Rebuild_Required`, `Blocked`, `Rejected`, `Human_Review_Required`.

These dimensions must not collapse.

### Legacy vocabulary policy
Any term that functionally competes with these dimensions becomes `legacy` or `deprecated`.

It may survive only as:
- historical alias,
- migration note,
- or backward-compatible reference.

It may not remain alive as an active parallel system.

## 9. Minimum admissibility standards
Visible admissibility is evaluated for three output families:
- valid output,
- valid recommendation,
- valid artifact.

These families share one common rule: mandatory binary gates govern minimum admissibility; auxiliary scores help rank, prioritize, and monitor, but do not replace structural judgment.

### 9.1 Valid output
A valid output must be admissible in content and representation. At minimum it must satisfy:
- explicit claim linkage,
- traceable context,
- clear intended use,
- coherence with regime and mode,
- honest representation,
- critical limits visible or traceable without material ambiguity,
- sufficient review,
- absence of destructive open conflict,
- adequacy to reader and format,
- and review or falsation condition when applicable.

### 9.2 Valid recommendation
A valid recommendation guides conduct. Therefore it requires a harder standard than a descriptive output.

At minimum it must include:
- explicit base claim,
- clear objective,
- visible critical dependency,
- coherent regime,
- change or reversibility condition,
- non-overstated language,
- and sufficient review.

### Critical distinction
Preliminary recommendation is not equivalent to high-weight recommendation.

Decision-grade mainly supports recommendations to:
- measure,
- prioritize,
- review,
- explore,
- sequence,
- and not close yet.

Verification-grade may support higher-weight recommendations, but not absolute prescriptions.

### 9.3 Valid artifact
A valid artifact must be:
- semantically explicit,
- subordinated to the base claim,
- safe against material overreading,
- and of real explanatory value.

At minimum it must include:
- claim linkage,
- clear purpose,
- explicit visual semantics,
- allowed interpretation,
- prohibited interpretation,
- visible limits,
- regime alignment,
- and sufficient review.

### Critical distinction
Explanatory value does not increase inherited epistemic strength.

### 9.4 Binary gates and auxiliary scores
Critical admissibility conditions are binary. If they fail, the output does not enter regardless of aggregate score.

Recurrent system gates:
- claim linkage,
- traceable context,
- intended use or clear objective,
- required review,
- honest representation,
- visible critical limits,
- absence of destructive open conflict,
- review condition when applicable,
- and regime alignment.

Legitimate auxiliary scores include, depending on object type:
- `output_validity_score`
- `content_validity_score`
- `representation_integrity_score`
- `traceability_completeness_score`
- `review_completeness_score`
- `overread_risk_penalty`
- `recommendation_support_score`
- `dependency_clarity_score`
- `recommendation_risk_score`
- `reversibility_score`
- `artifact_validity_score`
- `artifact_integrity_score`
- `visual_semantics_clarity_score`
- `interpretation_control_score`
- `artifact_explanatory_value_score`
- `artifact_overread_risk_penalty`
- `semantic_overreach_risk_score`
- `visual_density_penalty`

### Non-negotiable rule
An output, recommendation, or artifact does not enter by average score if a critical binary condition fails.

### 9.5 Final admissibility checklist
Every material output must answer explicitly, before being shown:
1. It has explicit claim linkage.
2. It has sufficient traceable context.
3. It has clear or inherited intended use.
4. It is coherent with the applicable regime.
5. It is coherent with the base claim's `lifecycle_status`.
6. It does not depend on destructive open conflict.
7. Representation is honest.
8. Critical limits are visible or traceable without material ambiguity.
9. Probable reading does not exceed real support.
10. It passed verification view or sufficient review.
11. It has review, change, or falsation condition when applicable.
12. Its format adds real explanatory value for this context.

Possible final outcomes:
- `Approved`
- `Approved_with_Warning`
- `Limit_Scope`
- `Rebuild_Required`
- `Blocked`
- `Rejected`
- `Human_Review_Required`

Scores order; the checklist decides minimum admissibility.

### Rule of final checklist subordination
The final checklist is not a second epistemic theory. It is a final visible release gate that consumes rules already defined in evidence, grammar, regimes, traceability, and minimum standards.

## 9.6 Epistemic Execution Model
The abstract execution philosophy of Phase 0 is:

**Generate first, govern second, publish last.**

The epistemic constitution must not operate as a total pre-generation muzzle. The system must be able to generate candidate material, tag it, govern it, and decide visibility at the end.

### 1. Generation layer
Function:
- produce candidate claims,
- hypotheses,
- recommendations,
- outputs,
- artifacts,
- explanations,
- and analytical narrative.

Inputs:
- observations,
- evidence,
- structured analytical output,
- constraints,
- assumptions,
- context,
- and analytical engine outputs.

Outputs:
- candidate claim-like units,
- candidate output-like units,
- candidate artifact-like units.

### 2. Epistemic tagging layer
Function:
- assign provisional classification to generated material.

Minimum outputs:
- `claim_family`,
- regime candidate,
- exploratory / Decision-grade / Verification-grade candidate,
- preliminary overread risk,
- `intended_use`,
- dependency structure,
- governance lane.

### 3. Analytical linkage layer
Function:
- bind generated material to reconstructible support.

Minimum outputs:
- link to evidence,
- link to structured analytical output,
- link to constraints,
- link to assumptions,
- link to Claim Objects,
- and dependency tree.

### 4. Review layer
Function:
- review semantic fidelity and representational discipline.

Minimum dimensions:
- semantic proportionality,
- regime consistency,
- representation honesty,
- dependency completeness,
- visibility of critical limits,
- probable overread risk.

Output of this layer:
- `review_outcome`

### 5. Admissibility layer
Function:
- apply binary gates,
- use auxiliary scores,
- assign visible state and final decision.

Minimum outputs:
- `publication_status`
- `admissibility_decision`

Rule:
- gates decide minimum admissibility;
- scores rank, prioritize, and monitor;
- failed critical gates are not compensated by average score.

### 6. Publication / action layer
Possible destinations:
- internal exploratory,
- visible Decision-grade output,
- visible Verification-grade output,
- approved with limits,
- blocked,
- rebuild required,
- human review required,
- retired or historical only.

### 7. Logging and propagation layer
Function:
- update traceable objects,
- update lifecycle,
- propagate effects to dependent outputs and artifacts,
- preserve auditable trail.

Rules:
- no silent overwrite;
- no material change without trace;
- no dependent object remains active by inertia when its base changed materially.

## 10. Anti-asphyxia clause
The framework does not exist to shut down valuable hypotheses under low information. It exists to label, delimit, trace, and govern them without selling them as confirmation.

### Operative rule
Decision-grade must remain able to produce:
- exploratory structural claims,
- serious prioritization,
- preliminary recommendations,
- useful outputs under partial support,
- and disciplined technical guidance,
provided they are not presented as strong closure.

### Adopted structural simplification
The same requirements do not apply with the same hardness to everything.
- `Lane_A`: exploratory / internal / low-impact; requires core gates, basic traceability, clear tagging, and proportional grammar.
- `Lane_B`: visible / serious Decision-grade; requires sufficient review, visible limits, clear dependency, and overreading control.
- `Lane_C`: Verification-grade / high-impact / report material; requires high traceability, strong representation control, canonical review outcome, and final visible admissibility decision.

- Useful early claims and outputs require core gates, not the full battery of verification demands.
- Harder conditions activate for:
  - high-weight recommendation,
  - verification claims,
  - visually persuasive artifacts,
  - and material outputs with high decisional, financial, or regulatory impact.

### Failure mode to prevent
Confusing epistemic governance with operative immobility.

## 11. Final operative rules

### It may enter serious reporting when
- the base claim is admissible for that use,
- the output is proportional to its support,
- traceability is sufficient,
- critical limits are visible or traceable,
- representation does not induce material overreading,
- and required review already occurred.

### It must remain exploratory when
- support still does not allow strong closure,
- causality remains open,
- domain is still partial,
- or the recommendation can only be preliminary.

### It must be blocked when
- claim linkage is missing,
- traceable context is missing,
- destructive open conflict exists,
- representation is misleading,
- required review did not occur,
- or the output depends on nonexistent or invented base.

### It must be limited when
- the claim remains admissible,
- but scope, caption, framing, visual, or recommendation exceed real support.

### It must be rebuilt when
- the base remains salvageable,
- but formulation, structure, or visual semantics must be redone.

### It must be rejected when
- it is structurally inadmissible for that use,
- it cannot be saved through reasonable limitation, degradation, or rebuild,
- or it violates fundamental framework rules.

### It must escalate to human review when
- cost of error is high,
- the recommendation has strong impact,
- the artifact has high persuasive power,
- multilayer conflict cannot be resolved semantically,
- or risk of misinterpretation remains material.

## 12.1 Boundary Between Phase 0 and Implementation
Phase 0 closes the epistemic constitution and the abstract execution model. It does not yet implement the full technical stack.

### Closed in Phase 0
- non-negotiable laws;
- critical distinctions;
- canonical taxonomies;
- canonical states;
- binary gates;
- auxiliary score families;
- canonical outcomes;
- governance lanes;

## 13. Controlled glossary
- `Operational truth`: validity sufficient for a delimited technical use without exceeding earned certainty.
- `Constrained agreement`: restricted agreement across independent layers, not narrative unanimity.
- `Decision-grade`: sufficient restriction to orient serious action without strong closure.
- `Verification-grade`: high restricted convergence sufficient for delimited high-weight technical assertion.
- `Claim`: governing unit of traceable assertion.
- `Output`: visible material formulation produced or reviewed by the LLM.
- `Artifact`: visual, tabular, or multimodal surface of interpretation.
- `Construction mode`: formulation.
- `Verification mode`: semantic judgment on formulation.
- `Validated`: technically useful claim with support sufficient for its use.
- `Verified`: delimited high-weight technical claim under strong convergence.
- `Lifecycle status`: logical state of the claim within the system.
- `Epistemic strength score`: quantified epistemic strength for serious operations.
- `Actionability score`: current utility for prioritizing or deciding.
- `Overread risk`: risk that probable reading exceeds real support.

## 14. Non-negotiable system laws
1. No single model is sovereign over truth.
2. No serious claim exists without traceability, validity conditions, and use-bounded certainty.
3. No formulation may semantically exceed real support.
4. No material output enters only because it looks good, sounds good, or has a high score.
5. No artifact may appear stronger than the claim on which it depends.
6. No serious recommendation enters without base claim, critical dependency, and change condition.
7. No material change occurs without trace, versioning, or dependent reevaluation.
8. No legitimate governance should kill disciplined exploration under incomplete evidence.
