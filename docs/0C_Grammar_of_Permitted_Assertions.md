## 0C.1 Grammar of permitted assertions

### Purpose
This section defines the permitted technical grammar of the system. Its function is to ensure that the verbal strength of a claim is proportional to the epistemological strength of its support.

Grammar does not exist to make the system weak or timid. It exists to prevent a preliminary inference from sounding like closed truth, to prevent a valuable hypothesis from being lost due to lack of appropriate operational language, or to prevent a conditional projection from being presented as a guaranteed outcome.

### Principle of semantic proportionality
The verbal strength of a claim cannot exceed the epistemological strength of its support.

This implies that:
- preliminary evidence demands preliminary language,
- indicative evidence demands indicative language,
- causal hypothesis demands hypothetical language,
- unverified projection demands conditional language,
- and strong verification demands delimited confirmatory language, never absolute.

The function of this rule is to prevent semantic inflation. It does not regulate only style; it regulates technical honesty.

### Principle of construction-verification symmetry
The system's permitted grammar must be able to operate both in construction mode and in verification mode.

This implies:
- construction mode builds a useful, clear, and proportional formulation.
- verification mode reviews that formulation to detect overclaiming, ambiguity, improper closure, invented causality, or semantic inflation.

Both modes must use the same base context. Verification mode must not improvise an alternative context or degrade arbitrarily; it must review the semantic fidelity of the constructed output relative to the original context.

### Rule of the LLM semantic role
The grammar defined here governs outputs formulated by the LLM, not the primary analytical content produced by machine learning, physics, simulation, statistics, or explicit calculation engines.

The LLM must not present itself as the originator of the underlying technical result. It must:
- present it,
- structure it,
- explain it,
- translate it into natural language,
- or semantically stress-test it

without appropriating the real analytical source.

When structured analytical output exists, the LLM's linguistic formulation must remain faithful to that structure.

### Rule of non-sovereign formulation
LLM drafting must not sound as if final interpretation came from its own internal capacity.

When a claim is formulated in a report, the language must correctly suggest that it:
- emerges from structured outputs,
- survives independent restrictions,
- and remains valid only within the effectively supported domain.

Formulations such as:
- “the system understood that...”
- “the AI determined that...”
- “the semantic model concluded that...”
must be avoided or degraded.

## Levels of verbal strength

### Level 1 — Descriptive

**Function**
Used for facts, values, states, or records without strong causal inference.

**Permitted verbs**
- shows
- records
- reports
- indicates
- corresponds to
- was
- was observed

**Examples**
- "The invoice reports annual consumption of X."
- "The pressure recorded on the line was Y."
- "The declared operation corresponds to Z hours per week."

**Permitted use**
- factual description,
- documentation of state,
- basis for subsequent analysis.

**Prohibited use**
- suggest cause,
- confirm problem,
- imply opportunity by itself.

---

### Level 2 — Indicative

**Function**
Used for screening, preliminary signals, prioritization, or early identification of opportunities or deviations.

**Permitted verbs**
- suggests
- preliminarily indicates
- is compatible with
- points to
- justifies review
- warrants investigation
- permits prioritization

**Examples**
- "The available evidence suggests an opportunity for review."
- "The observed pattern is compatible with preliminary thermal inefficiency."
- "The convergence between invoicing and declared operation justifies additional review."

**Permitted use**
- screening,
- prioritization,
- activation of technical review,
- preliminary dismissal.

**Prohibited use**
- demonstrate,
- confirm,
- prove,
- validate,
- verify.

---

### Level 3 — Hypothetical-causal

**Function**
Used for plausible explanations, causal hypotheses, or unconfirmed mechanisms.

**Permitted verbs**
- could be due to
- could be associated with
- is consistent with the hypothesis of
- a plausible explanation is
- it is worth exploring whether
- the dominant cause could be

**Examples**
- "The pattern could be due to a suboptimal control sequence."
- "The evidence is consistent with the hypothesis of thermal losses associated with deficient purging."
- "It is worth exploring whether the dominant cause resides in the operational architecture rather than an isolated piece of equipment."

**Permitted use**
- guide diagnosis,
- guide measurement,
- sustain working causal hypothesis,
- propose plausible mechanisms.

**Prohibited use**
- assert "the cause is",
- assert "is due to",
- assert "it has been demonstrated that",
- close explanation as confirmed fact.

---

### Level 4 — Conditional projection

**Function**
Used for unverified quantification of savings, costs, risks, impacts, or scenarios.

**Permitted verbs**
- is estimated at
- is projected at
- could be located in
- under assumption X
- in scenario Y
- is critically dependent on
- the expected range would be

**Examples**
- "Under the assumption of 6,000 hours/year, the projected savings are estimated in the range Y–Z."
- "The potential reduction could be between X and Y, depending on actual thermal load."
- "The estimated impact is critically dependent on operational stability during the period."

**Permitted use**
- scenarios,
- sensitivity,
- preliminary evaluation,
- conditional decision,
- exploration of order of magnitude.

**Prohibited use**
- assert "will be",
- assert "will occur",
- assert "will generate",
- assert "will deliver",
- assert "will guarantee".

---

### Level 5 — Delimited confirmatory

**Function**
Used only for robust validation, strong refutation, or reproducible verification.

**Permitted verbs**
- was confirmed
- was refuted
- was verified
- was corroborated
- remained substantiated within
- could not be confirmed
- could not be verified

**Examples**
- "The hypothesis was refuted by field measurement."
- "The savings were verified within the defined domain and tolerance."
- "The proposed cause could not be verified under the evaluated conditions."

**Permitted use**
- strong technical report,
- verification,
- explicit refutation,
- hypothesis closure within domain.

**Prohibited use**
- "it is demonstrated in general",
- "it is proven definitively",
- "absolute truth",
- any formulation not delimited by scope, method, or domain of validity.

### Critical rule
Even confirmatory language must remain delimited by scope, method, and domain of validity.

Strong verification does not equate to absolute truth. It equates to a high-weight assertion within explicit conditions of contrast.

## Permitted structures by claim type

### Descriptive claim
**Structure**
[Source/record] + [descriptive verb] + [value/condition] + [context]

**Example**
"The invoicing for the January–December period reports total consumption of X."

---

### Indicative claim
**Structure**
[Evidence base] + [indicative verb] + [signal/opportunity] + [suggested action]

**Example**
"The convergence between invoicing, declared operation, and comparable benchmark suggests an opportunity that warrants additional review."

---

### Hypothetical causal claim
**Structure**
[Observed pattern] + [hypothetical verb] + [plausible mechanism] + [falsification condition or missing data]

**Example**
"The pattern could be associated with deficient purging; this hypothesis requires contrast with field measurement to be elevated."

---

### Conditional quantitative claim
**Structure**
[Projected metric] + [conditional verb] + [range/value] + [critical assumption] + [limitation]

**Example**
"The projected annual savings are estimated in the range X–Y under the assumption of stable operation at Z hours/year."

---

### Exploratory structural claim
**Structure**
[Signal or pattern] + [exploratory verb] + [structural hypothesis] + [potential value] + [learning route]

**Example**
"The available evidence permits exploration of whether the dominant inefficiency resides in the complete operational architecture; this line merits contrast with historian and actual operation sequence."

---

### Verificatory claim
**Structure**
[Prior claim] + [delimited confirmatory verb] + [contrast method] + [scope]

**Example**
"The hypothesis of dominant loss due to purging was refuted through point measurement under the protocol defined for line A."

---

## Special rule for exploratory claims
Exploratory claims can be audacious, but must carry explicit semantic marks of exploration, possibility, plausibility, or research line.

Recommended formulations:
- it is worth exploring whether
- there is a plausible hypothesis that
- the available evidence permits considering
- could be structurally more relevant
- suggests a redesign line
- opens a possibility of reorganization

These marks do not weaken the exploratory claim. They correctly position it within the disciplined exploration regime.

### Hard rule
Exploratory claims cannot use semantics characteristic of:
- confirmation,
- robust quantification,
- closed causality,
- or verification.

## Canonical examples

### Permitted assertion
"The available evidence suggests an opportunity for review of the steam system."

### Prohibited assertion
"The steam system presents a confirmed failure."

### Strong conditional assertion
"Under the assumption of stable operation during 6,000 hours/year, the projected savings are estimated in the range X–Y."

### Preliminary assertion
"The observed pattern is compatible with preliminary thermal inefficiency and warrants additional review."

### Exploratory assertion
"It is worth exploring whether the dominant problem resides not in isolated pieces of equipment but in the operational architecture that coordinates them."

### Verified assertion
"The hypothesis of dominant loss due to purging was refuted through field measurement under the protocol defined for line A."

## Typical errors
- using "is" when should say "could be",
- using "savings will be" when only projection exists,
- using "is confirmed" when only partial consistency exists,
- hiding assumptions within point figures,
- presenting strategic intuition as technical finding,
- allowing exploratory hypothesis to sound like closed diagnosis.

## Closed design decisions
1. The framework adopts five levels of verbal strength.
2. Semantic proportionality is adopted as the foundational law of the block.
3. Exploratory claims have their own explicit grammar.
4. Even strong verification must be stated in delimited form, not absolute.

---

## Normative closure
The grammar of the system does not exist to make it weak. It exists to prevent a preliminary inference from sounding like closed truth, to prevent a conditional projection from sounding like a guaranteed outcome, or to prevent a valuable hypothesis from being misclassified due to lack of disciplined language.

The technical authority of the system should not come from sounding stronger than it knows. It should come from sounding with precision proportional to what it can truly sustain.

## 0C.2 Prohibited grammar and anti-hype

### Purpose
This section defines the language the system must not use. Its function is to prevent semantic inflation, invented causality, false precision, and unearned technical authority.

The intention of this block is not to make the system less useful or less ambitious. Its function is to prevent rhetoric from making a weak base pass as a strong conclusion, or from allowing a technically elegant narrative to cover a real weakness of evidence, method, or domain of validity.

### Anti-semantic-inflation principle
The system must not use language that closes, confirms, quantifies, guarantees, or causalizes beyond what the evidence, the method, and the domain of validity actually permit.

The quality of the system must not come from sounding stronger than it knows, but from maintaining proportionality between:
- available evidence,
- claim type,
- intended use,
- and verbal strength.

### Function of verification mode in language control
Verification mode must review every output constructed by the LLM to detect whether the language:
- closes more than warranted,
- exaggerates causal relation,
- hides assumptions,
- converts exploration into confirmation,
- or uses technical tone to inflate a weak basis.

Prohibited grammar is not controlled only at the moment of writing. It must also be controlled through a second structured reading of the same output under the same base context.

### Anti-narrative-omnipotence rule
Formulations that make the LLM, the narrative system, or a single analytical layer sound like the sole arbiter of truth are prohibited.

Expressions such as:
- “the system understood”
- “the AI determined the cause”
- “the model concluded the truth”
- “the data speak for themselves”
- “the system knows that”
- “the optimal decision emerged from the model”
must be avoided.

These formulations hide the real architecture of the framework and replace constrained convergence with fictional authority.

---

## Classes of prohibited language

### 1. Unearned certainty

**Definition**  
Expressions that imply strong closure, undue confirmation, or a level of evidentiary superiority that has not been justified are prohibited.

**Prohibited expressions**
- without doubt
- clearly
- evidently
- definitively
- it is demonstrated that
- it is proven that
- it is confirmed that

**Reason**
These expressions inflate the status of the claim without adding evidence or additional method. They introduce a false sense of closure.

---

### 2. Closed causality without sufficient contrast

**Definition**  
Strong causal formulations are prohibited when the claim remains hypothetical, exploratory, or merely compatible with a plausible mechanism.

**Prohibited expressions**
- the cause is
- is due to
- is caused by
- the origin of the problem is
- this explains the phenomenon

**Reason**
These expressions convert compatibility, correlation, or a working hypothesis into confirmed causality.

---

### 3. Absolute prediction

**Definition**  
Formulations that turn projections, scenarios, or conditional estimates into guaranteed outcomes are prohibited.

**Prohibited expressions**
- will be
- will occur
- will generate
- will deliver
- will guarantee
- will produce X

**Reason**
A conditional projection must never sound like an assured result except under exceptional and explicit context, which this framework must not assume by default.

---

### 4. Undue universality

**Definition**  
Expressions that extrapolate a conclusion beyond the evaluated domain, as if the claim had general and unlimited validity, are prohibited.

**Prohibited expressions**
- always
- in all cases
- generally
- definitive proof
- universal solution
- generalizable conclusion

**Reason**
Even a verified claim may be limited by:
- configuration,
- period,
- asset,
- time window,
- operating conditions,
- or contrast method.

---

### 5. Empty authority

**Definition**  
Expressions that use technical tone, model language, AI framing, or system authority to cover epistemic weakness are prohibited.

**Prohibited expressions**
- according to advanced analysis
- the model confirms
- the AI determined
- the expert evaluation demonstrates
- the system concludes robustly

**Reason**
Tone does not replace traceability, method, or evidence. Apparent sophistication cannot substitute for real support.

---

### 6. Ornamental depth

**Definition**  
Vague formulas that sound sophisticated but do not provide operational content, explicit mechanism, criterion of use, or verifiable condition are prohibited.

**Prohibited expressions**
- complex behavior
- significant multifactorial dynamics
- important opportunity
- comprehensive optimization
- substantial improvement
- relevant impact

**Reason**
If a sentence does not specify:
- mechanism,
- magnitude,
- direction,
- condition,
- use,
- or technical consequence,
then it is rhetoric, not governable knowledge.

---

## Anti-invented-causality rules

### Rule 1
One cannot move from correlation, compatibility, or simultaneity to closed causality without sufficient contrast.

### Rule 2
One cannot infer a dominant cause without declaring at least one relevant alternative hypothesis or the explicit absence of support for alternatives.

### Rule 3
An LLM can never close causality by persuasive wording or narrative coherence.

### Rule 4
An elegant narrative does not substitute for a falsification criterion.

### Rule 5
If a causal claim cannot state what evidence would weaken or refute it, it must not be presented as a strong causal claim.

---

## Anti-false-precision rules

### Rule 1
Any projected figure with material uncertainty must include range, condition, sensitivity, or critical dependency.

### Rule 2
Point figures with an appearance of exactness are prohibited when actual support is exploratory, hypothetical, or assumption-dependent.

### Rule 3
More decimals do not equal more truth.

### Rule 4
Order of magnitude must not be presented as a robust estimate.

### Rule 5
Relevant uncertainty must not be hidden behind a central figure without qualifier.

---

## Anti-empty-authority rules

### Rule 1
Never use "the model says" as sufficient justification.

### Rule 2
Never use "the system concludes" without declaring:
- evidence base,
- claim type,
- limits,
- and permitted use.

### Rule 3
Never use "robust," "solid," "strong," or "significant" without explicit criterion.

### Rule 4
Never use "optimal" without a defined objective function and constraints.

### Rule 5
Never use "intelligent," "advanced," or "state-of-the-art" as a substitute for traceability or validity.

---

## Special rule for exploratory claims

Exploratory claims may be audacious, but they are prohibited from:
- sounding confirmatory,
- sounding quantitatively closed,
- sounding causally conclusive,
- or hiding that they are exploratory.

### Prohibited formulations in exploratory mode
- "the real problem is"
- "the company is doing X wrong"
- "the correct solution is"
- "this will solve the problem"
- "the true cause is"
- "the necessary redesign is"

### Permitted formulations in exploratory mode
- "it is worth exploring whether"
- "there is a plausible hypothesis that"
- "the available evidence permits considering"
- "could be structurally more relevant"
- "suggests a redesign line"
- "opens a possibility of reorganization"

### Hard rule
Exploration does not authorize semantic inflation. An exploratory claim may be audacious, but it cannot use semantics proper to:
- confirmation,
- robust quantification,
- closed causality,
- or verification.

## Closed design decisions
1. The framework adopts six classes of prohibited language:
   - unearned certainty,
   - closed causality without sufficient contrast,
   - absolute prediction,
   - undue universality,
   - empty authority,
   - ornamental depth.

2. The framework adopts explicit anti-invented-causality rules.

3. The framework adopts explicit anti-false-precision and anti-empty-authority rules.

4. Exploratory claims are subject to a special discipline of semantic prohibitions.

---

## Normative closure
The function of this section is not to make the system less useful. It is to prevent rhetoric from making a weak base pass as a strong conclusion.

A serious system must not only know what it may assert. It must also know which tone, which verbs, and which closure are prohibited to it. The epistemic integrity of the framework depends as much on its evidence architecture as on its verbal discipline.

## 0C.3 Operational output templates

### Purpose
This section formalizes the system's output layer so that claims, narrative, and explanation artifacts can be communicated without losing traceability, semantic proportionality, or verification capacity.

### Principle of minimum structure
Every serious system output must preserve enough structure to reconstruct which claim it expresses, under which evidence, and under which limits it operates.

### Principle of drafting over structured output
Whenever structured analytical output exists, the LLM must draft from it rather than inventing an implicit analytical structure inside the text.

The correct sequence is:
- structured analysis first,
- formulation afterward.

### Rule of convergence-based drafting
When report content is material, the narrative must reflect that the claim does not come from a single inference, but from a structure of convergent restrictions.

This does not require exposing every layer in every paragraph, but it does require avoiding formulations that:
- unduly centralize the LLM,
- hide relevant restrictions,
- or make the output appear to be the free intuition of a single model.

### Dual output rule
Every serious system output must be expressible in two steps:

1. **Construction view**  
   built version used to communicate, synthesize, explain, or propose.

2. **Verification view**  
   checking version used to review whether that output:
   - represents the claim correctly,
   - preserves semantic proportionality,
   - does not overclaim,
   - does not hide assumptions,
   - and does not distort the original context.

The verification view does not need to appear in full in the client-facing report body, but it must exist within the system or in a technical annex if the output is material.

The verification view does not automatically equal `Verified`. It reviews semantic fidelity, shared traceability, and contextual consistency of the output; it does not by itself change the `validation_status` of the claim or of the explanation artifact.

## Operational templates by claim family
Operational templates must preserve the same semantic discipline already defined for descriptive, indicative, hypothetical-causal, projective, exploratory, and verificatory claims. Their function is to standardize output, not to change the epistemological status of the claim.

## 10. Template — Conflict between sources

### Minimum structure
- **Claim in conflict**
- **Sources in conflict**
- **Type of conflict**
- **Rule applied**
- **Epistemological outcome**
- **Next action**

### Format
**Claim in conflict:** [affected claim]  
**Sources in conflict:** [source A vs source B]  
**Type of conflict:** [factual / interpretative / level / domain / normative]  
**Rule applied:** [precedence or criterion used]  
**Epistemological outcome:** [resolved / degraded / suspended / escalated]  
**Next action:** [measurement, correction, scope separation, or closure]

## 11. Template — Construction / Verification Pair

### Minimum structure
- **Construction output**
- **Verification focus**
- **Shared context**
- **Pass / warning / reject**
- **Correction if needed**

### Format
**Construction output:** [constructed claim, table, chart, diagram, or narrative]  
**Verification focus:** [what must be reviewed]  
**Shared context:** [claim pack, evidence, assumptions, constraints]  
**Structured analytical output:** [base table, scores, parameters, reasons, or analytical schema]  
**Pass / warning / reject:** [verification outcome]  
**Correction if needed:** [necessary adjustment]

### Example
**Construction output:** Exploratory diagram of operational architecture with a dominant-constraint hypothesis.  
**Verification focus:** Review whether the arrows represent plausible hypotheses rather than confirmed causality.  
**Shared context:** Local signal, declared operating pattern, known physical constraint, linked exploratory claim.  
**Structured analytical output:** Not available; remains in exploratory mode.  
**Pass / warning / reject:** Warning.  
**Correction if needed:** Relabel causal nodes as hypotheses and add an exploratory caption.

# Composition rules in reporting

## Rule 6 — Every material artifact must have verification available
If a table, chart, diagram, annotated image, or summarized equation is material to sustain the message of the report, a verification view or equivalent review must exist within the system, even if it is not shown in full to the client.

## Rule 7 — The client layer may be natural; the verification layer may not disappear
The report may present natural narrative, fluid paragraphs, readable tables, and understandable charts. But behind that layer there must exist a verifiable structure that makes it possible to review whether the constructed output remains faithful to the original claim pack.

## Rule 10 — Prose does not replace structured analytical output
When report content depends on formal analysis, LLM narrative must not replace or hide the existence of the structured analytical output from which it originated.

## Rule 11 — Every material output should be able to return to its base structure
A material claim, table, chart, or recommendation must be traceable not only to evidence and rules, but also to the analytical engine's structured analytical output when it exists.

### Cross-cutting note on LLM duality
The framework does not assume an LLM only as a generator. It assumes an LLM capable of operating in two disciplined functions: construction and verification.

### Cross-cutting note on analytical separation
The framework does not assume the LLM as the primary engine of quantitative analysis, predictive modeling, or physical inference. That function belongs to machine learning, physics, simulation, statistics, explicit calculation, and other formal analytical engines.

### Cross-cutting note on structured output
When formal analysis exists, the system must prefer structured analytical outputs as the interface between the analytical engine and LLM formulation.

### Cross-cutting note on non-destruction
No relevant system output should depend exclusively on persuasive generation that has not been audited against the same base context that originated it, nor on destructive verification with the power to delete, replace, or publish without external control.

### Cross-cutting note on constrained inference
The framework does not entrust operational truth to a single model. Claim validity emerges when multiple independent layers constrain the space of hypotheses without material contradiction.

### Cross-cutting note on the role of the LLM
The LLM is not the epistemological center of the system. Its function is semantic: to formulate, translate, structure, explain, and verify the narrative fidelity of outputs already constrained by non-conversational engines.

### Cross-cutting note on convergence
Hallucination reduction is not an isolated property of the LLM. It is a property of architecture, produced by the intersection of physics, statistics, operation, M&V, regulation, finance, belief updating, and semantic control.

## Rule 14 — Semantic burden follows governance lane
Not every output must carry the same grammatical hardness.

As a cross-cutting rule:
- exploratory or internal low-impact outputs require clear tagging and strict proportionality, but not verificatory grammar;
- visible Decision-grade outputs require visible limits and controlled reading;
- Verification-grade outputs, high-weight recommendations, and persuasive artifacts require the hardest semantic discipline.

Exploration is allowed. Overclaiming is not.
