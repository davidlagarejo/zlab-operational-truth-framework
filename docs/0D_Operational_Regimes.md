## 0D.1 Formal definition of Decision-grade

### Purpose
This section defines the **Decision-grade** regime as the framework's operational standard for producing useful intelligence, actionable claims, and outputs oriented toward prioritization, sequencing, discard, or next-step design under still-incomplete but disciplined, traceable, and semantically proportional evidence.

Its function is to avoid two symmetric failures:
- paralysis until perfect evidence or strong verification closure is available,
- and early semantic inflation that makes useful orientation pass as unearned verification.

### Formal definition of Decision-grade
A claim, result, or system output enters the **Decision-grade** regime when it has traceable evidence, explicit transformation rules, sufficient structured analytical output where applicable, declared validity conditions, and adequate semantic control to sustain a delimited technical use of orientation, prioritization, or next-step design without exceeding the certainty actually justified.

Decision-grade:
- does not equal strong verification,
- does not equal definitive technical closure,
- does not equal closed bankability,
- does not equal confirmed M&V,
- does not equal robust validation equivalent to audit,

but it does authorize useful intelligence under disciplined uncertainty in order to:
- orient action,
- structure the next step,
- discriminate hypotheses,
- prioritize opportunities or risks,
- and organize further investigation without pretending strong confirmation.

### Function of the regime
Decision-grade exists to allow the framework to produce operational value before reaching Verification-grade, provided that:
- minimum evidence is sufficient for the intended use,
- grammar is proportional,
- limits are explicit,
- and the output does not invade unearned verificatory territory.

It is the regime in which the system may:
- reduce uncertainty materially,
- produce useful claims for prioritization,
- detect relevant opportunities or constraints,
- structure plausible hypotheses,
- and design the next technical move.

It does not exist to close technical discussion. It exists to orient with integrity.

### Rule of sufficient partial restriction
Decision-grade is not defined because the LLM “can already say something useful.” It is defined because there is already enough multilayer restriction to orient action, even though the strong convergence required by Verification-grade does not yet exist.

In this regime:
- some layers may still be incomplete,
- some restrictions may still be soft,
- and some legitimate exploration may still be allowed.

But the output must no longer be free. It must be restricted enough to orient preliminary decision without invading verificatory territory.

### Minimum admissible inputs
Decision-grade requires, as a reasonable combination depending on claim type and intended use:

- minimum relevant local evidence,
- sufficient structured analytical output when formal analysis exists,
- explicit screening, derivation, or transformation rules,
- declared assumptions,
- known physical, normative, or operational constraints,
- minimum operational context,
- traceable claim pack,
- and absence of unresolved material conflict that destroys the intended use.

It does not require strong contrast sufficient for Verification-grade, but it does require enough basis for the orientation to be technically serious and auditably defensible within its domain.

### Structured analytical output within Decision-grade
Within Decision-grade, complete formal analysis will not always exist. The regime admits that in some cases the system operates with:
- local signals,
- partial evidence,
- explicit screening rules,
- initial comparisons,
- or plausible structural hypotheses.

However, when formal analysis produced by machine learning, physics, simulation, statistics, explicit rules, deterministic calculation, or other formal analytical engines exists:
- that analysis must first produce structured analytical output,
- that output must precede LLM formulation,
- and the LLM must write and verify on top of that structured basis.

In Decision-grade, the absence of structured analytical output does not automatically invalidate every output, but it does limit:
- permitted verbal force,
- quantification robustness,
- the type of admissible recommendation,
- and the ability to sustain higher-weight material claims.

### Legitimate outputs
Within Decision-grade, the following are legitimate outputs if they satisfy semantic proportionality and sufficient traceability:

- descriptive claims,
- comparative claims,
- indicative / screening claims,
- causal-hypothesis claims,
- conditioned quantitative projection claims,
- exploratory structural claims,
- preliminary recommendations,
- conflict notes,
- suspended claims,
- exploratory or indicative explanation artifacts,
- technically disciplined natural narrative,
- actionable tables,
- exploratory diagrams,
- and explicit next-step proposals.

Also legitimate are outputs that do not close the claim, but do organize uncertainty usefully in order to decide:
- where to measure,
- what to compare,
- what to discard,
- what to review first,
- or which hypothesis deserves further contrast.

### Prohibited outputs
Within Decision-grade, the following are prohibited outputs:

- verified savings,
- confirmed cause,
- strong refutation,
- closed bankability,
- robust validation equivalent to audit,
- proven compliance,
- confirmed M&V,
- universal conclusions outside domain,
- any claim using strong confirmatory semantics without sufficient contrast,
- any output presenting exploratory claims as technical closure,
- and any conditioned projection presented as fact.

Decision-grade does not authorize strong verification semantics for narrative, aesthetic, or commercial convenience.

### Construction mode within Decision-grade
Construction mode within Decision-grade may construct:

- working hypotheses,
- useful syntheses,
- actionable tables,
- exploratory diagrams,
- indicative explanation artifacts,
- natural narrative,
- preliminary comparisons,
- conditioned projections,
- and next-step proposals.

Its function in this regime is to:
- formulate with useful clarity,
- preserve traceability,
- declare conditions and limits,
- separate evidence, inference, hypothesis, and recommendation,
- and produce outputs that help action without overclaiming.

Construction mode in Decision-grade may be bold in exploration, but it may not:
- present plausibility as confirmation,
- sell screening as closed diagnosis,
- nor use visuals or figures to simulate unearned robustness.

### Verification mode within Decision-grade
Verification mode within Decision-grade must review every constructed output using the same base context, claim pack, evidence, constraints, and available structured analytical output.

It must review especially whether:
- language exceeds the evidence,
- the claim invades unearned verificatory territory,
- the diagram exaggerates exactness or causality,
- the number appears more robust than it is,
- the output loses operational common sense,
- the chart appears to show nonexistent precision,
- or the claim should be degraded, suspended, or corrected.

Within Decision-grade, verification mode may:
- approve,
- approve with warning,
- degrade,
- reject,
- or request explicit correction.

It may not directly edit the original output.

Verification mode within Decision-grade does not exist to destroy legitimate exploration. It exists to prevent exploration, synthesis, or projection from disguising themselves as strong technical closure.

### Rule of technical responsibility
Decision-grade authorizes serious orientation, not strong technical closure.

This implies that the system may:
- prioritize,
- compare,
- orient review,
- sequence action,
- or design the next step,

but it must not make that regime pass as:
- closed audit,
- final robust validation,
- strong confirmation,
- nor high-demand technical guarantee.

The regime's technical responsibility consists in orienting with discipline, not in closing with rhetorical excess.

### Rule of exploratory value
Decision-grade is the regime in which the system's exploratory value lives most strongly.

It may legitimately host:
- structural hypotheses,
- plausible redesigns,
- high-leverage systemic readings,
- tentative causal explanations,
- and non-obvious opportunities under low data,

provided that:
- they remain labeled exploratory or conditioned when applicable,
- they do not disguise themselves as verification,
- they do not use unearned confirmatory grammar,
- and they declare what evidence would elevate, degrade, or discard them.

The framework must not kill exploratory value through excessive notarial rigidity. It must govern it semantically.

### Format rule in Decision-grade
Decision-grade allows multiple forms of explanation:

- short technical paragraphs,
- prioritization tables,
- signal/evidence/action tables,
- comparative charts,
- exploratory diagrams,
- simplified system maps,
- impact-vs-uncertainty matrices,
- or natural narrative without additional visual support.

Format selection must not be automatic. It must depend on whether that format truly improves:
- legibility,
- compression,
- interpretation,
- or decision.

In this regime, clarity for action orientation weighs more than template rigidity.

### Main risks
The main risks of Decision-grade are:

- early overclaiming,
- inflated exploration,
- false projection,
- visual overreach,
- overreading weak signals,
- false equivalence between comparison and confirmation,
- and paralysis by excess rigor when enough basis actually exists to orient action.

Decision-grade must avoid both premature hype and unproductive timidity.

### Criterion of use
Decision-grade should be used only when the following conditions occur simultaneously:

- minimum evidence sufficient for the intended use,
- sufficient traceability between source, transformation, and output,
- grammar proportional to the real strength of support,
- acceptable passage through verification mode,
- legitimate action orientation,
- and no invasion of unearned verificatory territory.

If these conditions are not met, the claim must:
- be degraded,
- suspended,
- reclassified,
- or remain as an internal exploratory note without serious-claim status.

### Summary table

| Dimension | Decision-grade |
|---|---|
| Purpose | orient prioritization, sequencing, discard, or next-step design under incomplete but disciplined evidence |
| Evidence | minimum relevant and sufficient for the intended use, without requiring final strong contrast |
| Structured analytical output | desirable; mandatory as formulation basis when sufficiently available formal analysis exists |
| Permitted claims | descriptive, comparative, indicative/screening, causal-hypothesis, conditioned quantitative projection, exploratory structural claims, preliminary recommendations, conflict notes, suspended claims |
| Prohibited claims | verified savings, confirmed cause, strong refutation, closed bankability, robust validation equivalent to audit, proven compliance, confirmed M&V, universal conclusions outside domain |
| Permitted formats | natural narrative, short technical paragraphs, tables, comparative charts, exploratory diagrams, simplified maps, impact/uncertainty matrices, according to contextual explanatory value |
| Main risk | early overclaiming or, at the opposite extreme, paralysis by excess rigor |
| Verification mode | controls semantic excess, apparent unearned robustness, visual overreach, loss of common sense, and need for degradation or correction |

### Canonical examples

#### Example 1 — Valid: serious prioritization without confirmation
**Claim:** The convergence between billing, declared operation, and observed load pattern justifies prioritizing review of the steam system before other intervention lines.  
**Why it is valid:** it supports serious prioritization, uses indicative language, and does not confirm cause or savings.

#### Example 2 — Valid: exploratory structural claim
**Claim:** There is a plausible structural hypothesis that the dominant constraint does not lie in an isolated asset but in the full operating sequence; it is worth contrasting this before optimizing individual assets.  
**Why it is valid:** it preserves high exploratory value, declares plausibility, and does not present itself as confirmed diagnosis.

#### Example 3 — Invalid: sounds confirmatory
**Claim:** The main problem has already been confirmed and is due to deficient blowdown-system control.  
**Why it is invalid:** it uses confirmatory causal closure without a proof standard compatible with Decision-grade.

#### Example 4 — Invalid: uses projection as fact
**Claim:** The intervention will generate $120,000 in annual savings.  
**Why it is invalid:** it presents a conditioned projection as an assured fact.

### Normative closure
Decision-grade is the operational regime in which the framework may produce useful intelligence, serious claims, and actionable orientation before reaching strong verification, without collapsing into paralysis or semantic inflation.

In this regime:
- underlying analysis still belongs to evidence and formal analytical engines, not to the LLM,
- structured analytical output must precede formulation when formal analysis exists,
- construction mode may explore, synthesize, and propose,
- verification mode must control semantic excess and degrade when needed,
- and the system may orient seriously without pretending definitive technical closure.

No Decision-grade without minimum relevant evidence, traceability, proportional grammar, acceptable verification pass, and legitimate action guidance within bounded certainty.

## 0D.2 Formal definition of Verification-grade

### Purpose
This section defines the Verification-grade operational regime as the standard under which the framework may sustain high technical-weight assertions within an explicitly delimited domain. Its function is to establish which claims, contrasts, quantifications, refutations, and verificatory artifacts are legitimate when strong convergence between independent layers of restriction already exists.

Verification-grade does not exist to produce an appearance of total certainty. It exists to enable robust validation, technical audit, serious decision support, and delimited verificatory assertion without exceeding certainty actually earned.

### Formal definition of Verification-grade
Verification-grade is the operational regime in which a claim can be sustained with high technical weight because it has strong and traceable evidence, sufficiently robust structured analytical output when formal analysis exists, explicit contrast method, delimited validity domain, high reconstructibility, and sufficient multilayer convergence between independent restrictions, without unresolved material contradiction.

Verification-grade:
- does not equal absolute truth,
- does not equal universal truth,
- does not equal system omniscience,
- does not equal the LLM “verifying better” by itself,
- and does not equal epistemological authority of a single layer.

It does equal, instead, a form of delimited and defensible technical closure for more demanding uses, when the assertion already survives strongly enough under simultaneous contrast between empirical evidence, structured analysis, and relevant heterogeneous restrictions.

### Function of the regime
Verification-grade exists to sustain assertions more demanding than Decision-grade when the system no longer only orients action, but can defend a strong claim within a delimited scope.

Its function is to enable, depending on the case:
- delimited confirmation,
- delimited non-confirmation,
- strong within-domain refutation,
- serious contrast between competing hypotheses,
- much better-supported quantification,
- serious support for audit or external review,
- and higher technical-weight recommendations.

It does not exist to eliminate all residual uncertainty. It exists to prevent the framework from confusing delimited closure with universality.

### Rule of strong convergence
Verification-grade does not depend only on having “more evidence.” It depends on the claim surviving without material contradiction across multiple independent layers of restriction.

At minimum, a verification-grade claim must withstand, when applicable:
- observation and strong empirical evidence,
- sufficient structured analytical output,
- physical consistency,
- explicit uncertainty quantification,
- operational coherence,
- verificatory comparability and contrast discipline,
- normative admissibility,
- financial viability for the intended use,
- and strict semantic review of the final output.

No single model is trusted with truth. Truth emerges from constrained agreement across independent layers.

Accordingly, Verification-grade cannot be attributed to:
- an isolated measurement that contradicts physics, comparability, or domain,
- a sophisticated model that does not survive uncertainty or real operation,
- a structured output that does not converge with independent restrictions,
- nor a technically impeccable narrative that does not survive multilayer conflict.

### What it requires beyond Decision-grade
Beyond Decision-grade, Verification-grade requires simultaneously:

1. a higher proof standard for the intended use;
2. stronger, more relevant, and more traceable empirical evidence;
3. more robust, granular, and reconstructible structured analytical output;
4. greater convergence across independent layers of restriction;
5. explicit contrast method and clear derivation rules;
6. lower dependence on unverified critical assumptions;
7. lower tolerance for unresolved material conflict;
8. greater density of traceability and auditable reconstruction;
9. greater language discipline and explicit delimitation of scope;
10. greater technical responsibility for what is asserted, what is not asserted, and the exact domain of validity.

The jump from Decision-grade to Verification-grade is not produced by stronger narrative. It is produced by a stronger support structure.

### Minimum admissible inputs
Verification-grade requires, as a sufficiently strong combination for the claim and intended use:

- relevant and traceable empirical evidence;
- robust structured analytical output when formal analysis exists;
- explicit contrast method;
- clearly identified calculation, derivation, or comparison rules;
- declared validity conditions;
- delimited validity domain;
- absence of unresolved material conflict;
- uncertainty quantification when applicable;
- sufficient verificatory comparability when applicable;
- and auditable reconstruction capacity by a competent third party.

If this combination does not exist with sufficient strength, the claim must not be presented as Verification-grade.

### Structured analytical output within Verification-grade
Within Verification-grade, the LLM cannot compensate with drafting for what is missing in analytical structure.

When formal analysis exists and is produced by machine learning, physics, simulation, statistics, explicit rules, deterministic calculation, or other formal analytical engines:
- that analysis must first produce structured analytical output;
- that output must precede verificatory formulation;
- that output must semantically dominate the subsequent formulation;
- and every piece of LLM drafting must be traceable back to that structured base.

That output must be:
- traceable,
- sufficiently granular,
- reconstructible,
- consistent with sources and method,
- adequate to the level of exigence of the claim,
- and sufficient to sustain serious audit or technical review.

Verificatory formulation must not compensate with prose for what was not analytically earned.

### Multilayer convergence within Verification-grade
Verification-grade is the regime in which multilayer convergence is already strong enough to sustain delimited closure of high technical weight.

This explicitly implies that:
- a good isolated measurement is not enough if it contradicts physics, comparability, uncertainty, or validity domain;
- a good model is not enough if it does not survive real operation, empirical conflict, or assumption review;
- strong quantification is not enough if it does not withstand method, traceability, or multilayer consistency;
- impeccable narrative is not enough if it does not survive independent restrictions.

Verificatory robustness emerges when multiple heterogeneous restrictions remain simultaneously compatible without material contradiction:
- strong empirical evidence,
- structured analysis,
- physics,
- statistics,
- operation,
- M&V or equivalent verificatory discipline,
- regulation,
- finance when applicable,
- and strict semantic review.

None of these layers, by itself, possesses interpretive sovereignty.

### Legitimate outputs
Within Verification-grade, the following are legitimate outputs, provided they maintain semantic proportionality, high traceability, and explicit domain:

- robust descriptive claims;
- robust comparative claims;
- strongly supported causal-hypothesis claims;
- quantitative projection claims with strong support and explicit limitation;
- delimited verification / confirmation claims;
- delimited non-confirmation claims;
- delimited refutations;
- higher technical-weight recommendations;
- verificatory explanation artifacts;
- contrast tables and charts;
- executive summaries faithful to actual contrast;
- structured methodological annexes;
- and auditable technical annexes.

Also legitimate are outputs that do not universalize closure, but do clearly establish what remained:
- confirmed,
- not confirmed,
- refuted,
- conditioned,
- or domain-limited.

### Prohibited outputs
Within Verification-grade, the following are prohibited outputs:

- absolute or universal language;
- assertions without delimited domain;
- total closure when unresolved material conflict persists;
- strong quantification without sufficient structured analytical output;
- explanation artifacts that appear more closed than actually earned;
- automatic extrapolation to other assets, periods, climates, or configurations;
- use of “Verified” as a synonym for “true in general”;
- unearned total causality;
- rhetorical elimination of residual uncertainty;
- and any formulation that makes the LLM, an isolated model, or a single layer sound like the sole arbiter of truth.

### Construction mode within Verification-grade
Construction mode still exists within Verification-grade, but no longer as a space for free exploration. Its function is to build outputs of high communicational rigor from a strong analytical base that is already sufficiently constrained.

It may construct:
- delimited verificatory claims;
- contrast tables;
- measurement and comparison charts;
- auditable explanation artifacts;
- high-weight technical narrative;
- executive summaries faithful to actual contrast;
- summarized equations;
- and structured methodological annexes.

Construction mode within Verification-grade must:
- preserve strict fidelity to the analytical base;
- declare scope, method, limits, and domain;
- not expand semantically beyond earned closure;
- not convert strong contrast into universality;
- and not use communicational clarity to inflate the epistemological status of the claim.

### Verification mode within Verification-grade
Verification mode within Verification-grade must be especially strict.

At minimum, it must review whether:
- the contrast was overread;
- the claim sounds more universal than warranted;
- the result is presented as absolute when it is only delimited;
- the table or chart appears more closed than it truly is;
- the narrative hides relevant limits;
- the validity domain remained implicit instead of explicit;
- non-confirmation was disguised as near-confirmation;
- quantification sounds more robust than permitted;
- or the final formulation unduly centralizes the LLM or a single analytical layer.

It must remain explicit that LLM `verification mode` does not automatically equal the epistemological status `Verified`. The former describes a function of semantic and contextual review. The latter, when applicable, describes a final epistemological status of the claim under a strong contrast standard.

Within Verification-grade, verification mode may:
- approve,
- approve with warning,
- degrade,
- reject,
- or request explicit correction.

It must not directly edit the original output.

### Technical responsibility of the regime
Verification-grade authorizes delimited high technical-weight assertion, not technical absolutism.

The regime's technical responsibility consists in asserting only that which:
- has been sufficiently contrasted,
- withstands relevant multilayer convergence,
- maintains high traceability,
- declares method,
- declares domain,
- declares limits,
- and preserves semantic honesty proportional to the support actually achieved.

### Format rule in Verification-grade
Verification-grade may tolerate greater technical density than Decision-grade, but that density must not become saturation by rigid template.

It may legitimately use:
- precise technical narrative;
- contrast tables;
- verificatory charts;
- auditable diagrams;
- measured-vs-expected or before-vs-after charts;
- summarized equations;
- verificatory explanation artifacts;
- methodological annexes;
- and multimodal combinations.

Format selection still depends on contextual explanatory value. A denser format is legitimate only if it improves understanding, reconstruction, comparison, or auditability without artificially inflating claim strength.

### Main risks
The main risks of Verification-grade are:

1. overreading the contrast;
2. undue generalization;
3. excessive semantic closure;
4. misleading visual rigidity;
5. confusing non-confirmation with total refutation;
6. confusing verification mode with `Verified`;
7. hiding residual uncertainty behind technical language;
8. allowing a single layer to appear interpretively sovereign;
9. and extrapolating a local closure to uncontrasted domains.

### Criterion of use
A Verification-grade output is admissible only when the following conditions occur simultaneously:

1. strong evidence sufficient for the intended use;
2. adequate structured analytical output when formal analysis exists;
3. explicit contrast method;
4. declared validity domain;
5. high traceability and auditable reconstruction;
6. absence of unresolved material conflict;
7. sufficient multilayer convergence between relevant independent restrictions;
8. delimited, non-absolute confirmatory grammar;
9. lower dependence on unverified critical assumptions;
10. and resistance to strict verification mode without semantic collapse.

If these conditions are not met, the output must:
- be degraded,
- be suspended,
- be reclassified,
- or be reopened as hypothesis, partial validation, or conditioned decision.

### Summary table

| Dimension | Verification-grade |
|---|---|
| Purpose | sustain high technical-weight assertions within a delimited domain through strong convergence across independent layers |
| Evidence | strong, relevant, traceable, and sufficiently contrasted for the intended use |
| Structured analytical output | expected and more strongly required when formal analysis exists; it must precede and govern verificatory formulation |
| Multilayer convergence | central condition; the claim must survive without material contradiction across evidence, structured analysis, physics, statistics, operation, M&V, regulation, finance when applicable, and strict semantic review |
| Permitted claims | robust descriptive, robust comparative, strongly supported causal-hypothesis, delimited strong quantification, verification / confirmation, non-confirmation, delimited refutation, higher technical-weight recommendations |
| Prohibited claims | absolutism, universality, closure with unresolved material conflict, strong quantification without sufficient structured basis, extrapolation outside domain, false equivalence between `Verified` and general truth |
| Permitted formats | technical narrative, contrast tables, verificatory charts, auditable diagrams, summarized equations, methodological annexes, and multimodal combinations according to contextual explanatory value |
| Main risk | overreading the contrast and converting delimited closure into unearned universal authority |
| Verification mode | strict, non-destructive, oriented toward detecting overassertion, false universality, exaggerated domain, visual overreach, and loss of limits |

### Canonical examples

**Valid — delimited verified claim**  
“The dominant-loss hypothesis associated with blowdown was refuted through point measurement under the protocol defined for Line A, under the observed operating conditions and within the evaluated domain.”

**Valid — non-confirmation**  
“The proposed mechanism could not be confirmed with the available evidence under the evaluated conditions and the applied contrast method.”

**Invalid — absolute language**  
“The true cause of the problem was definitively demonstrated.”

**Invalid — extrapolation outside domain**  
“The result verified in Line A demonstrates that the entire plant presents the same dominant cause.”

**Valid — well-bounded verificatory visual artifact**  
“Contrast chart between measured signal and expected signal under the evaluated mechanism, limited to the measurement campaign, with domain, method, tolerance, and validity conditions explicitly stated in the technical caption.”

### Normative closure
Verification-grade does not equal universal truth. It equals a high technical-weight assertion that is defensible, contrasted, and delimited within explicit conditions of evidence, method, domain, and traceability.

Its legitimacy does not emerge from the authority of a single layer, nor from the LLM, nor from stronger narrative. It emerges from sufficiently strong constrained convergence between independent layers that survive without material contradiction for the intended technical use.

## 0D.3 Dual regimes by mode

### Purpose
This section defines the LLM's operational duality within the framework and fixes how its two formal modes operate:
- `construction mode`
- `verification mode`

Its function is to establish precisely:
- what each mode does,
- what it cannot do,
- how it interacts with Decision-grade and Verification-grade,
- and why these modes do not equal epistemological states of the claim.

This section does not redefine operational truth, evidence hierarchy, or claim taxonomy. It defines only the disciplined way in which the LLM participates in the production and semantic review of outputs already governed by evidence, structured analytical output, restrictions, and context.

### Formal definition of Construction Mode
`construction mode` is the operational mode in which the LLM formulates, organizes, drafts, summarizes, structures, or semantically represents an output from evidence set, structured analytical output, assumptions, constraints, claim family, intended use, domain of validity, and epistemological status already available in the system.

In this mode, the LLM may produce:
- narrative text,
- structured claims,
- recommendations,
- tables,
- charts,
- diagrams,
- captions,
- explanation artifacts,
- summaries,
- client-facing versions,
- and technical or methodological annexes.

`construction mode` is not a primary analytical engine. It cannot:
- invent non-existent analytical basis,
- replace missing structured analytical output with prose,
- elevate claim epistemological strength by itself,
- nor bypass restrictions imposed by evidence, physics, statistics, regulation, finance, operation, or validity domain.

Its function is to construct semantically useful formulation over an already constrained base, not to create technical truth by narrative style.

### Formal definition of Verification Mode
`verification mode` is the operational mode in which the LLM reviews an already constructed output using the same base context with which that output was produced.

Its function is to review whether the output:
- overasserts,
- loses restrictions,
- semantically distorts the claim,
- represents support in a misleading way,
- appears more precise than warranted,
- introduces dangerous ambiguity,
- hides relevant limits,
- or loses operational common sense.

`verification mode` may:
- approve,
- approve with warning,
- degrade,
- reject,
- request explicit correction,
- or escalate to human review.

It cannot:
- directly edit the original,
- delete,
- overwrite without trace,
- arbitrarily change the problem,
- invent alternative context,
- nor become the silent final author of the output.

Its function is to produce judgment on output fidelity, not to invisibly replace the act of construction.

### Master rule
`construction mode` produces formulation.

`verification mode` produces judgment on the fidelity of that formulation.

Neither one, by itself, substitutes for epistemological validation of the underlying claim.

This implies that:
- a good output in `construction mode` does not automatically convert the claim into `Validated` or `Verified`,
- and an output reviewed in `verification mode` does not by itself alter `validation_status` if the epistemological base has not changed.

These modes govern the LLM's operational participation. They do not, by themselves, govern the technical truth of the claim.

### Rule of same base context
`construction mode` and `verification mode` must operate over the same base context.

That base context must include, at minimum:
- `evidence_set`,
- `structured_analytical_output`,
- `assumptions`,
- `constraints`,
- `claim_family`,
- `intended_use`,
- `domain_of_validity`,
- and `current_epistemic_status`.

Accordingly:
- `verification mode` cannot degrade or reject by inventing alternative context not present in the original package;
- it cannot review against a different problem;
- it cannot introduce implicit new evidence;
- and it cannot change the evaluation domain without making that explicit as escalation or reopening of the case.

If genuinely new context appears, it is no longer the same review of the same output, but a new round of construction and verification over an updated claim pack.

### Cross-cutting rule of governance proportionality
Not every output should carry the same governance intensity.

The framework operates, at minimum, with three implicit governance-load lanes:
- `Lane_A`: exploratory / internal / low-impact; requires basic traceability, clear labeling, and proportional grammar;
- `Lane_B`: visible / serious Decision-grade; requires sufficient semantic review, visible limits, and clear dependency;
- `Lane_C`: Verification-grade / high-impact / report material; requires high traceability, harder gates, stronger control of artifacts and recommendations, and reinforced visible admissibility.

The heaviest controls must concentrate in `Lane_C`, not punish by default the legitimate early value of `Lane_A`.

### Canonical model of `review_outcome`
Semantic review results are normalized under one dimension:
- `Pass`
- `Pass_with_Warning`
- `Limit_Required`
- `Rebuild_Required`
- `Reject`
- `Human_Review_Required`

Actions such as degrade to exploratory, suspend claim, or require explicit limits may still exist, but they must be treated as corrective actions or governance effects, not as a parallel family of outcomes.

### Matrix of dual regimes

#### Decision-grade / Construction
In this combination, `construction mode` operates within a regime in which multilayer restriction is already sufficient to orient serious action, but strong convergence of verificatory type does not yet exist.

It may legitimately construct:
- screening claims,
- comparative claims,
- causal hypotheses,
- conditioned quantitative projections,
- exploratory structural claims,
- prioritization tables,
- exploratory diagrams,
- useful narrative,
- and preliminary recommendations.

It must maintain:
- proportional grammar,
- explicit limits,
- visible assumptions,
- separation between evidence, hypothesis, and recommendation,
- and semantic honesty relative to the actually available support.

The dominant risk here is:
- early semantic inflation.

#### Decision-grade / Verification
In this combination, `verification mode` reviews outputs constructed under Decision-grade.

It must review especially whether the output:
- exaggerates the evidence,
- converts hypothesis into confirmation,
- presents figures as robust without sufficient support,
- visualizes structure too exactly,
- loses operational common sense,
- or presents exploratory claims as if they were technical closure.

Admissible review outcomes in this intersection:
- `Pass`
- `Pass_with_Warning`
- `Limit_Required`
- `Rebuild_Required`
- `Reject`
- `Human_Review_Required`

Here `verification mode` protects against hype, semantic inflation, and early overreading, but does not yet require strong verificatory standard equivalent to Verification-grade.

#### Verification-grade / Construction
In this combination, `construction mode` operates over a base already strengthened by strong evidence, explicit method, robust structured analytical output when applicable, high traceability, and sufficiently strong multilayer convergence.

It may legitimately construct:
- delimited verificatory claims,
- non-confirmation claims,
- delimited refutations,
- contrast tables,
- measured-vs-expected charts,
- auditable explanation artifacts,
- high technical-weight narrative,
- methodological annexes,
- and executive summaries faithful to the contrast.

It must remain disciplined:
- it cannot universalize closure,
- it cannot expand domain,
- it cannot convert delimited contrast into general truth,
- nor can it make the LLM appear as the primary source of technical closure.

The dominant risk here is:
- overreading the contrast,
- and false universality.

#### Verification-grade / Verification
In this combination, `verification mode` reviews outputs constructed under Verification-grade.

It must review especially whether the output:
- sounds more universal than warranted,
- hides limits or domain,
- transforms non-confirmation into total refutation,
- uses charts that appear excessively closed,
- formulates as definitive something only delimitably verified,
- or unduly centralizes the LLM or a single layer as arbiter of the result.

Admissible review outcomes in this intersection:
- `Pass`
- `Pass_with_Warning`
- `Limit_Required`
- `Rebuild_Required`
- `Reject`
- `Human_Review_Required`

Here `verification mode` controls verificatory overextension, false generalization, and excessive semantic closure.

### Outputs permitted by mode

#### Construction Mode
`construction mode` may produce:
- narrative text,
- structured claims,
- recommendations,
- tables,
- charts,
- captions,
- explanation artifacts,
- summaries,
- client-facing versions,
- technical annexes,
- and methodological annexes.

It may also produce:
- `construction_output_vN`,
- semantically equivalent wording variants,
- client/annex pairs,
- and visual or tabular representations when they provide contextual explanatory value.

#### Verification Mode
`verification mode` may produce:
- admissibility judgment,
- warnings,
- degradations,
- rejection,
- explicit correction request,
- signaling of missing limits,
- signaling of conflict with context,
- signaling of dangerous ambiguity,
- signaling of overassertion,
- and assessment of misinterpretation risk.

It may also produce a structured verification view of the type:
- result,
- reason,
- detected conflict,
- missing limit,
- required action,
- and escalation criterion if applicable.

### Outputs prohibited by mode

#### Construction Mode
`construction mode` cannot:
- feign non-existent evidence,
- close unearned causality,
- suppress claim limits,
- appropriate analysis not performed by the LLM,
- compensate with prose for absence of structured analytical output,
- nor inflate claim strength through format, tone, or visual density.

#### Verification Mode
`verification mode` cannot:
- directly edit the original,
- overwrite without trace,
- change the base context,
- turn into the silent final author,
- artificially resolve unresolved cross-layer conflicts,
- nor degrade arbitrarily by introducing criteria not present in the claim pack or framework rules.

### Rule of no direct editing
The minimum governed sequence must be:

1. `construction_output_vN`
2. `verification_review_vN`
3. `decision_on_output`
4. `correction_requested_if_any`
5. `construction_output_vN+1`
6. new review if applicable

Correction must always remain as a new act of construction.

Accordingly:
- no “invisible correction” exists inside `verification mode`,
- no silent overwrite of the output exists,
- and every material change after review must remain traceable as a newly constructed version.

### Escalation rule
The system must escalate to human review when:
- the output is material for strong decision,
- the verifier detects relevant misinterpretation risk,
- conflict exists between layers that semantic review cannot resolve,
- or the cost of poor formulation is high.

This applies especially to:
- verificatory claims,
- high-weight recommendations,
- causal diagrams,
- operational architecture,
- comparisons with potential financial or regulatory impact,
- claims with risk of executive overreading,
- and outputs whose formulation may alter a relevant technical decision.

`Human_Review_Required` does not imply automatic epistemological failure of the claim. It implies that the operational risk of poor formulation or poor reading exceeds what should be resolved only through automatic semantic review.

### Correct relation with `Validated` and `Verified`
`construction mode` and `verification mode` are operational modes of the LLM.

`Validated` and `Verified` are epistemological states of the claim.

They must not collapse into one another.

This implies that:
- a claim may be `Unvalidated` and be reviewed in `verification mode`;
- a claim may be `Verified` and be drafted in `construction mode`;
- an output may pass satisfactorily through `verification mode` and still remain only `Decision-grade`;
- and a poorly formulated output may require correction even when the underlying claim already has strong support.

Correct examples:
- `Unvalidated` claim in `verification mode`: an exploratory hypothesis reviewed to prevent it from sounding confirmatory.
- `Verified` claim in `construction mode`: a refutation already won through robust measurement that the LLM drafts into a delimited executive summary.

`output_mode` describes how the LLM participates.
`validation_status` describes the epistemological state of the claim.
Both axes must remain separate.

### Summary table

| Regime / mode | Function | What it can do | Main risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Decision-grade / Construction | formulate useful outputs under sufficient partial restriction | screening claims, comparative claims, causal hypotheses, conditioned projections, exploratory structural claims, prioritization tables, exploratory diagrams, useful narrative, preliminary recommendations | early semantic inflation |
| Decision-grade / Verification | review useful outputs without yet requiring strong verificatory closure | `Pass`, `Pass_with_Warning`, `Limit_Required`, `Rebuild_Required`, `Reject`, `Human_Review_Required`; later corrective actions may still degrade or suspend as needed | letting screening pass as diagnosis or projection as fact |
| Verification-grade / Construction | formulate outputs of high communicational rigor over strong base | delimited verificatory claims, non-confirmation, delimited refutations, contrast tables, measured-vs-expected charts, auditable explanation artifacts, technical narrative, methodological annexes, faithful executive summaries | overreading the contrast and false universality |
| Verification-grade / Verification | control verificatory overextension and improper closure | `Pass`, `Pass_with_Warning`, `Limit_Required`, `Rebuild_Required`, `Reject`, `Human_Review_Required`; later corrective actions may still limit scope, degrade, or rebuild | converting delimited closure into general truth or total refutation |

### Canonical examples

#### Example 1 — Valid: Decision-grade / Construction
**Output:** “The convergence between billing, declared operation, and comparable benchmark suggests an opportunity that justifies additional review of the steam system.”  
**Correct reading:** useful, indicative, traceable, and semantically proportional screening claim.  
**Why it is valid:** it orients serious action without invading verificatory territory.

#### Example 2 — Valid: Decision-grade / Verification with warning
**Construction output:** “The intervention will generate savings close to $120,000 annually.”  
**Verification result:** `Pass_with_Warning` if corrected into explicit conditioned projection; otherwise it should be rebuilt or degraded.  
**Reason:** the figure sounds too robust for the available support and hides critical assumptions.  
**Expected correction:** new act of construction expressing range, conditions, and dependence on assumptions.

#### Example 3 — Valid: Verification-grade / Construction
**Output:** “The dominant-loss hypothesis associated with blowdown was refuted through point measurement under the protocol defined for Line A, under the observed operating conditions and within the evaluated domain.”  
**Correct reading:** delimited verificatory claim.  
**Why it is valid:** it uses strong base, explicit method, delimited domain, and non-absolute confirmatory grammar.

#### Example 4 — Valid: Verification-grade / Verification rejecting overextension
**Construction output:** “The verified result definitively demonstrates the real cause of the problem across the whole plant.”  
**Verification result:** `Reject` or `Limit_Required`.  
**Reason:** it universalizes a local contrast, hides domain, and transforms delimited verification into general truth.

#### Example 5 — Exploratory diagram degraded for looking like confirmed causality
**Construction output:** operational architecture diagram with solid causal arrows and caption suggesting that the full sequence has already been confirmed as the dominant cause.  
**Verification result:** `DEGRADE_TO_EXPLORATORY` or `REJECT`, depending on severity.  
**Reason:** the visual artifact appears to show confirmed causality when support authorizes only exploratory structural hypothesis.  
**Required action:** new act of construction with explicit relabeling of hypotheses, visible limits, and non-confirmatory semantics.

### Normative closure
Construction and verification modes govern how the LLM participates inside the framework. They do not by themselves determine the technical truth of the claim nor substitute the epistemological status of underlying support.

Their function is to make the output:
- semantically faithful,
- operationally clear,
- epistemologically disciplined,
- and proportional to the real level of evidence, structured analytical output, restrictions, and validity domain.

`construction mode` builds formulation.
`verification mode` reviews formulation.
Neither one, by itself, equals `Validated` or `Verified`.
The technical truth of the claim continues to emerge from constrained convergence across independent layers, not from the mere fact that the LLM constructed or reviewed an output.

## 0D.4 Rule of transition from Decision-grade to Verification-grade

### Purpose
This section defines what triggers, what justifies, and what does not justify the transition of a claim from Decision-grade to Verification-grade. Its function is to prevent a change in drafting, format, documentary density, or narrative confidence from being confused with a real change of epistemological regime.

The transition exists to govern when a claim stops being supported mainly by preliminary support, serious screening, or disciplined exploration, and becomes supported strongly enough to sustain high technical-weight assertions within a delimited domain.

### Formal definition of transition
The transition from Decision-grade to Verification-grade is a formal change in the claim's epistemological regime. It occurs when the claim ceases to depend mainly on preliminary support, screening, or disciplined exploration, and becomes sufficiently supported by strong evidence, robust structured analytical output, explicit contrast method, multilayer convergence, and high traceability to sustain high technical-weight assertions within a delimited domain.

It is not a change of style. It is not a change of tone. It is not a presentation upgrade. It is a change in the claim's real support structure.

### Principle of no semantic promotion
No claim rises by better drafting.

No claim rises:
- by more tables,
- by better visualization,
- by more documentary density,
- by a more convincing LLM,
- by better prose,
- by a more confident narrative,
- nor by greater formal elegance of the hypothesis.

A claim rises only when its epistemological conditions materially change.

More presentation does not equal more verification.

### Legitimate transition triggers
The following are legitimate triggers of transition, provided they materially change the claim's support structure:

1. new or stronger empirical evidence;
2. more robust, more granular, or more reconstructible structured analytical output;
3. explicit contrast or comparison method previously absent or insufficient;
4. greater multilayer convergence among evidence, structured analysis, physics, statistics, operation, regulation, M&V, and finance when applicable;
5. material reduction of critical assumptions;
6. material reduction of unresolved conflict;
7. sufficient traceability for technical defense, audit, or demanding review.

The transition is not justified by a single isolated indicator if the rest of the relevant layers remain insufficient or conflictive.

### Non-triggers of transition
The following do not constitute valid justifiers of promotion:

- more text,
- better prose,
- better visualization,
- more tables,
- more references,
- more subjective confidence,
- more convincing LLM output,
- more elegant hypothesis,
- irrelevant documentary accumulation,
- narrative reordering,
- nor formal density without material change in support.

More presentation does not equal more verification.

### Minimum threshold for promotion
A claim may be promoted from Decision-grade to Verification-grade only when the following conditions occur simultaneously:

1. sufficiently strong evidence for the intended use;
2. structured analytical output adequate to the new level of exigence;
3. explicit contrast or comparison method;
4. material reduction of critical assumptions;
5. absence of unresolved material conflict;
6. delimited validity domain;
7. grammar compatible with delimited high technical-weight assertion;
8. resistance to strict verification mode without semantic collapse.

If one of these conditions fails materially, promotion must not be treated as earned.

### Rule of partial promotion
Promotion does not have to be binary. It may be partial, localized, or restricted to one dimension of the claim.

This implies that:
- quantification may rise while causality does not;
- non-confirmation may rise while alternative explanation does not;
- Line A may rise while the whole plant does not;
- performance contrast may rise while mechanism hypothesis does not;
- or one part of the claim may rise in technical scope while another remains in Decision-grade.

In these cases, the system must explicitly declare:
- which part rose,
- which part did not rise,
- and which part remains open.

No semantic carryover is allowed for claim components that did not reach the same epistemological threshold.

### Rule of inverse degradation
Promotion is not irreversible.

Verification-grade is not a permanent medal. A promoted claim may be degraded again if any of the following appears:
- measurement error,
- rupture of validity domain,
- new contradictory evidence,
- use outside scope,
- visual or narrative overextension,
- belief update against the claim,
- previously unseen multilayer conflict,
- or loss of acceptability of the contrast method used.

Inverse degradation is not a system anomaly. It is a normal consequence of an architecture that governs claims by real support rather than narrative prestige.

### Relation with Construction / Verification Modes
`construction mode` may propose that a claim now deserves promotion evaluation.

`verification mode` may review whether that proposed promotion is semantically compatible with the available technical base.

But the real promotion is not decided by the LLM alone.

Promotion occurs only if the underlying claim satisfies the corresponding epistemological threshold.

LLM verification mode does not promote by itself; it only verifies whether the proposed promotion is semantically compatible with the available technical base.

This implies that:
- an output may be impeccably written and still remain in Decision-grade;
- an output may pass verification mode and still not deserve promotion;
- and promotion requires material change of support, not only formal correction of the output.

### Criterion of use
The transition rule must be applied whenever there is an intention to elevate a claim from serious orientation to high technical-weight assertion.

The transition is admissible only when:
- the claim's support structure materially changes,
- convergence across independent layers improves,
- claim fragility decreases,
- sufficient traceability for technical defense appears,
- and the new level of language would no longer be misleading relative to the available support.

If these conditions are not met, the claim must remain in Decision-grade, even if its formulation is excellent.

### Summary table

| Dimension | Transition rule |
|---|---|
| What triggers it | new or stronger evidence, more robust structured analytical output, explicit contrast method, greater multilayer convergence, lower dependence on assumptions, lower conflict, greater traceability |
| What does not trigger it | more text, better prose, better visualization, more tables, more references, more subjective confidence, more convincing LLM output, more elegant hypothesis, irrelevant documentary accumulation |
| Minimum threshold | sufficiently strong evidence, adequate structured analytical output, explicit method, material reduction of critical assumptions, absence of material conflict, delimited domain, compatible grammar, resistance to strict verification mode |
| Partial promotion | allowed; must declare which part rises, which does not, and which remains open |
| Reversibility | total; a promoted claim may be degraded if its support or domain materially changes |
| Role of construction mode | may propose promotion evaluation and build formulation compatible with the new level if support justifies it |
| Role of verification mode | reviews whether the proposed promotion is semantically compatible with the available technical base; it does not promote by itself |
| Main risk | confusing presentation improvement with real improvement of epistemological support |

### Canonical examples

#### Case 1 — Legitimate promotion
A claim initially issued as Decision-grade about dominant loss in Line A receives:
- new robust point measurement,
- stronger structured analytical output,
- explicit contrast against competing hypotheses,
- reduction of critical assumptions,
- and domain delimited to observed operating conditions.

**Result:** legitimate promotion to Verification-grade for that delimited claim.

#### Case 2 — No promotion
The team produces:
- better narrative,
- new tables,
- clearer visuals,
- and a more convincing executive summary,

but adds no new strong evidence, no superior structured analytical output, and resolves no material conflict.

**Result:** no promotion. The claim remains in Decision-grade.

#### Case 3 — Partial promotion
A causal hypothesis and a preliminary quantification coexist in the same case. Quantification gains:
- better contrast method,
- better structured analytical output,
- and sufficient traceability.

The alternative causal explanation, however, still depends on critical assumptions and unresolved conflict.

**Result:** delimited quantification rises; causality does not. Promotion is partial and must be declared as such.

#### Case 4 — Inverse degradation
A claim promoted to Verification-grade for a specific line is later presented as valid for the whole plant. Subsequently there appears:
- rupture of validity domain,
- contradictory evidence in other lines,
- and narrative overextension of the original closure.

**Result:** inverse degradation. The claim no longer retains Verification-grade at that expanded scope.

### Normative closure
The transition from Decision-grade to Verification-grade is not a change of tone. It is a change of epistemological regime.

That change is legitimate only when the claim's support structure materially improves, multilayer convergence increases, support fragility decreases, and sufficient traceability appears to sustain high technical-weight assertion within a delimited domain.

The transition is not produced by narrative persuasion. It is produced by real improvement in evidence, method, structured analytical output, convergence, and reconstructibility.

### Cross-cutting note on constrained inference
The framework does not entrust operational truth to a single model. Claim validity emerges when multiple independent layers constrain the space of hypotheses without material contradiction.

### Cross-cutting note on the role of the LLM
The LLM is not the epistemological center of the system. Its function is semantic: to formulate, translate, structure, explain, and verify the narrative fidelity of outputs already constrained by non-conversational engines.

### Cross-cutting note on convergence
Hallucination reduction is not an isolated property of the LLM. It is a property of architecture, produced by the intersection of physics, statistics, operation, M&V, regulation, finance, belief updating, and semantic control.
